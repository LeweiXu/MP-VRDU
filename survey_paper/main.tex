\begin{abstract}
Real-world documents routinely span tens to hundreds of pages, yet most visually rich document understanding (VRDU) research and surveys remain anchored to the single-page setting. We argue that multi-page VRDU (MP-VRDU) is not an incremental extension but a distinct problem defined by a single pressure: answer-bearing evidence is sparsely distributed across pages that exceed any backbone's context window, forcing every system to decide what evidence to retain, discard, or iteratively acquire. Through this evidence-management lens, we systematically review 36 representative MP-VRDU systems and organise the field along four axes: architectural family (page-to-document encoding, backbone-centric adaptation, retriever-generator pipelines, agentic pipelines), multi-page multimodal representation, inference-time adaptation, and training strategy. We further consolidate MP-VRDU datasets and surface a contamination loop in which benchmark PDFs dominate fine-tuning corpora. Finally, we identify open challenges in efficiency, faithfulness, evidence pipelines, supervision integrity, and reproducible evaluation that define the MP-VRDU frontier.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Visually-rich documents (VRDs) such as scientific papers, business reports, financial filings and technical manuals are pervasive in real-world workflows, and understanding them is a long-standing problem at the intersection of NLP and computer vision. Most prior work has focused on the single-page (SP) setting, where each input is a self-contained page, and the field has matured into a well-defined sub-area with established benchmarks. However, real-world documents are rarely single-page, with contracts spanning dozens of pages, and scientific papers referencing figures and tables many pages apart. Multi-page visually rich document understanding (MP-VRDU) is not a simple extension of SP-VRDU, since total document length frequently exceeds the backbone's context window, evidence is sparsely distributed across pages, and document structure such as section hierarchies and cross-references cannot be recovered from per-token layout signals alone.

MP-VRDU has seen rapid growth recently, with new architectures, training strategies, and inferencing methods emerging. Existing work spans architectural extensions of single-page document transformers~\cite{tito2023hierarchical, blau2024gram}, adaptations of pretrained multimodal LLMs~\cite{duan2025docopilot, hu2025mplug2}, retrieval-augmented pipelines~\cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag}, and increasingly agent-based systems~\cite{sun2025docagent, wang2025vidorag}, often combined with task-specific training or prompted reasoning loops~\cite{xiong2026docr1, han2025mdocagent}. However, this body of work has developed along largely parallel lines, with limited cross-family comparison and inconsistent terminology, and few works explicitly distinguish what is genuinely MP-distinctive from what is inherited from the single-page setting. As a result, the field lacks a unified view of the design choices that matter most in multi-page settings, motivating the need for systematic organisation.

Prior surveys have charted VRDU from complementary angles: OCR-centric deep learning pipelines~\cite{subramani2020survey}, multimodal pretraining~\cite{ding2026deep}, deep learning for information extraction over VRDs~\cite{gbada2025deep}, and recent LLM-era treatments~\cite{ding2025survey} including multimodal retrieval-augmented generation for long documents~\cite{gao2025scaling}. While valuable, these works remain anchored to the single-page setting, treating multi-page documents as an incremental extension rather than a distinct problem. As a result, the concerns that define MP-VRDU receive no systematic treatment: how architectures manage cross-page evidence under context-window constraints; how text, visual, and structural (especially cross-page) modalities trade page coverage against reading fidelity; how inference-time retrieval, reasoning, and agentic orchestration coordinate; how training strategies adapt retrievers, reasoners, and orchestration policies for document-scale supervision; and how LLM-synthesised data shapes both training and evaluation integrity.


This survey provides a systematic account of the emerging MP-VRDU frontier. First, we define MP-VRDU as a problem distinct from SP-VRDU and organise existing systems through an evidence-management perspective, covering page-to-document encoding, backbone-centric adaptation, retriever-generator pipelines, and agentic pipelines (§\ref{sec:architecture}). Second, we analyse how multi-page systems represent and trade off text, visual, and structural modalities under document-length constraints (§\ref{sec:modality}). Third, we review inference- and training-time strategies for scaling MP-VRDU, focusing on how systems retrieve, reason over, and learn from cross-page evidence under long-context constraints. (§\ref{sec:inference-time}--§\ref{sec:training}). Finally, we consolidate MP-VRDU datasets and evaluation practices, and identify key open challenges in data scarcity, benchmark contamination, scalability, grounding, reliability, and reproducible evaluation (§\ref{sec:datasets}--§\ref{sec:challenges}).

\section{Methodology and Scope}
\label{sec:methodology}
This survey covers MP-VRDU systems published in the past 3 years across major venues such as CVPR, ACL, EMNLP and ICLR. Candidates were identified through keyword using terms such as ``\textit{multi-page document}'' and ``\textit{long document understanding}'' supplemented by forward-citation sweeps from anchor models and datasets. We manually screened candidate papers for genuine multi-page technique or pipeline contributions and evaluation on at least one multi-page dataset. Peer-reviewed publications were prioritised, with preprints retained only for clear architectural or training novelty. The dataset catalogue covers datasets used by the surveyed systems, supplemented with a small number of relevant but underused MP-VRDU datasets. LLM-assisted information extraction was followed by carefully human verification. Full search terms and selection criteria are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:methodology}.

\section{MP-VRDU Architecture Overview}
%\textcolor{red}{I think we need add a paragraph in appendix to describe general LLM in MP-VRDU. The performance table can add some general domain LLM as well. }
\label{sec:architecture}

Prior works have been organised primarily by OCR dependency, distinguishing OCR-based and OCR-free models~\citep{ding2025survey}. This axis is largely orthogonal to the central question in MP-VRDU, where systems differ chiefly in how cross-page evidence is selected, retained, and integrated across pages that exceed any single backbone's context window. Through this evidence-management lens, the field has shifted from encoding all pages jointly toward increasingly selective and iterative evidence handling, producing four recurring architectural trends, which are page-to-document encoding models, backbone-centric adaptation, retriever-generator pipelines, and agentic pipelines.

%Previous VRDU surveys \cite{ding2025survey} categorise models by OCR dependency, separating OCR-dependent models that consume text and layout boxes from OCR-free models that consume document images directly \cite{huang2022layoutlmv3}. This taxonomy is less informative in the multi-page setting, as OCR-using models often use extracted text as just one component in a multimodal pipeline \cite{sun2025docagent, wang2025vidorag}, while OCR-free models are often pretrained or aligned with OCR-derived supervision \cite{tanaka2025vdocrag, hu2024mplug}. The defining challenge in MP-VRDU is instead how a system manages document-scale evidence while bounded by context window limits. Answer-bearing evidence is typically sparse, distributed across pages, and multimodal in nature, so models must decide what information to retain, what to discard, and how the surviving information should interact across pages. These decisions are governed by architectural design, which determines how evidence is selected, preserved, aggregated, and exposed for reasoning. We therefore organise multi-page frameworks primarily by architectural trend, with OCR dependency as a secondary descriptor.

\subsection{Page-to-document Encoding Models}
\label{sec:arch-p2d}
Page-to-document encoding models process each page through a shared layout-aware encoder such as LayoutLMv3~\cite{huang2022layoutlmv3}, that fuses OCR text, bounding-box layout, and visual features within the page, then connect per-page representations through cross-page attention~\cite{borchmann2025arctic}, per-page summary tokens~\cite{tito2023hierarchical}, or recurrent state propagation~\cite{dong2024multi}.By preserving page boundaries and cross-page relations within the architecture, this design provides a strong inductive bias for documents with regular structural templates. 
However, this design requires all pages to be jointly encoded in a single forward pass, and its page-to-document compression can discard fine-grained evidence as input length increases~\cite{blau2024gram}.
% \textbf{Page-to-Document Encoding Models} encode each page through a shared document encoder including page level OCR text, layout, and visual features, then connect page representations through a document-level exchange mechanism such as summary tokens \cite{}, document-global tokens \cite{}, recurrent memory \cite{}, or sparse cross-page attention \cite{}. Cross-page interaction occur within the framework in a single forward pass, while page identity, local layout, and reading order remain explicit, providing a strong inductive bias for multi-page structure. Explicit cross-page modelling makes these models well suited to tasks where relevant evidence appears across pages within a coherent document structure, such as reports and business documents \cite{tito2023hierarchical, borchmann2025arctic}. Its limitations follow from the same design, as document-level representations may lose-fine grained evidence when page information is compressed or weakly connected \cite{blau2024gram, dong2024multi}. Moreover, computation grows with document length as all pages typically need to be encoded, making very long documents difficult to scale.

\subsection{Backbone-Centric Adaptation}
\label{sec:arch-bc}
Backbone-centric adaptation models directly process the document inside a pre-trained MLLM whose context window absorbs cross-page information directly. Adaptation strategies range from training only lightweight projectors~\cite{tanaka2024instructdoc} or visual-token compression modules~\cite{hu2025mplug2}, to fine-tuning the backbone itself through continued pre-training~\cite{duan2025docopilot}, instruction tuning, or reinforcement learning~\cite{xiong2026docr1}. This design benefits directly from the MLLM's instruction-following and reasoning capabilities. However, the backbone's context length now caps total document size, fine-grained signals depend on the fidelity of token compression~\cite{jia2024leopard}, and document structure such as section hierarchy and figure-caption linkage must be recovered implicitly from pixels~\cite{hannan2025docslm}, limits that motivate moving evidence selection out of the backbone altogether.

% This family adapts a pretrained MLLM backbone to multi-page input by training projectors that map per-modality features into the backbone's token space \cite{tanaka2024instructdoc}, adaptors that enable parameter-efficient fine-tuning for document understanding \cite{zhu2025laytokenllm}, and compression modules that contain visual token growth on long inputs \cite{hu2025mplug2}. Some works further unfreeze the backbone for continued pretraining or instruction tuning on document corpora, trading parameter efficiency for stronger document-specific grounding \cite{duan2025docopilot}. These models inherit the instruction-following and multimodal reasoning of the foundation model and remain parameter-efficient to train, fitting tasks that combine document reading with open-ended reasoning and moderate-length documents where long-context tuning massively improves MLLM performance on multi-page documents \cite{hu2024mplug, yu2024texthawk2}. However, as the backbone must absorb document-scale evidence internally, performance degrades beyond moderate-length documents, leaving quality bound to context length and to how faithfully fine-grained text and visual signal survive token compression \cite{jia2024leopard}. Additionally, reasoning over visual elements depends entirely on the backbone's pretrained visual capabilities, and as most models in this family are OCR-free, reliable text recovery requires high-resolution input that aggressive downsampling readily compromises. Moreover, document structure such as section hierarchy and figure-caption linkage must likewise be recovered implicitly from pixels rather than supplied through layout tokens or a structural encoder \cite{hannan2025docslm, duan2025docopilot}.

\subsection{Retriever-Generator Pipelines}
\label{sec:arch-rg}
Retriever-generator pipelines first select compact evidence units, such as chunks, pages, or graph nodes, and then generate answers conditioned on them~\cite{cho2024m3docrag,tanaka2025vdocrag}. Unlike backbone-centric models, which must fit the entire document into the model context, retriever-generator pipelines pass only selected pages or chunks to the generator, allowing them to handle longer documents. The retrieval and generation stages may be implemented by single modules or distributed across multiple specialised components~\cite{wang2024knowledge,duan2025m2rag,han2025mdocagent}, but the overall control flow remains a fixed pipeline executed once per query, which exposes the retrieved evidence as a partial audit trail. However, the design assumes that relevant evidence is rankable by query similarity in a single retrieval step, so evidence missed at retrieval is unrecoverable at generation, and questions that require aggregating multiple weakly relevant pages frequently fail~\cite{wu2025molorag}; performance is further bounded by upstream component quality, since chunking, OCR, and modality mismatches propagate directly into the answer.
%, pressures that motivate replacing the fixed pipeline with adaptive, multi-step reasoning.

% Retriever-generator systems divide MP-VRDU into evidence selection and answer generation, where a retrieval module returns a compact subset of OCR chunks, pages, visual patches, multimodal elements, or graph nodes, and a generator answers conditioned only on those units \cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag}. Inference cost depends on the retrieved content rather than document length, allowing this architecture to scale to documents that exceed any current MLLM context window. Modular decomposition allows the retriever and generator to be swapped independently while retrieved evidence provides a partial audit trail for the answer. Furthermore, the same interface extends from a single document to a corpus, supporting multi-document question answering without significant architectural change. Structured domains with reliable retrieval anchors such as scientific papers and technical manuals benefit most \cite{shin2025multidocfusion}, and visually rich pages benefit further when page-level visual retrieval preserves figures, tables, and layout that OCR-only pipelines lose \cite{tanaka2025vdocrag, xie2024wukong}. However, evidence missed at retrieval is unrecoverable at generation, and broadening the retrieved set to compensate introduces noise and attention dilution. Additionally, performance depends on parser and embedding quality, while chunking errors, OCR mistakes, and retriever-generator modality mismatches propagate directly into the answer. The single-pass design also presupposes that relevant evidence is rankable by query similarity, which weakens when answers require aggregating pages that are not individually strongly relevant \cite{wang2024knowledge, wu2025molorag}.

\subsection{Agentic Pipelines}
\label{sec:arch-agentic}
Agentic pipelines replace the fixed retrieval pipeline with iterative evidence acquisition: one or more LLM controllers issue successive tool calls, for retrieval, navigation, cropping, or OCR, and each subsequent decision is conditioned on the outcomes of earlier observations~\cite{zheng2026docvstar,sun2025docagent}. Roles such as inspection, synthesis, and adjudication may be distributed across heterogeneous backbones~\cite{yu2025mact,wang2025vidorag}, but the defining property of an agentic system is that its reasoning trajectory adapts to question difficulty rather than following a fixed control flow; multi-component systems with predetermined execution order, even when described as ``agents'', remain within the retriever-generator family. By lifting the single-retrieval-step bottleneck of retriever-generator systems, this design allows compute to scale with question difficulty rather than document length and exposes the trajectory as an inspectable audit trail. However, the variable-length action sequence makes inference cost difficult to bound before execution, controllers remain sensitive to prompt phrasing and may commit early to wrong evidence with silent failure, reproducibility is complicated by stochastic generation, and the system inherits the recall ceiling of every retriever, parser, or OCR module it calls.
% Recent surveys \cite{plaat2025agentic, wang2024survey} characterise agentic LLM systems through their capacity to reason, act, and interact with an external environment beyond a single forward pass. Following this framing, we categorise MP-VRDU systems that perform multi-pass evidence acquisition or distributes work across specialised agents as agentic, while reserving the retriever-generator family for single-pass models. Agentic systems thus treat MP-VRDU as an iterative evidence-seeking process, where one or more controller MLLMs decide what to inspect next from intermediate observations and issue tool calls for retrieval, navigation, cropping, or OCR. Roles such as retrieval, inspection, synthesis, and adjudication may be distributed across agents, with each agent backed by a different backbone model selected for its subtask \cite{han2025mdocagent} rather than tied to a single generator in retriever-generator systems. This design lifts the single-pass bottleneck, allows compute to scale with question difficulty rather than document length, and the trajectory itself becomes an inspectable audit trail. This suits questions whose evidence is distributed across pages or modalities and multi-hop questions that need query reformulation between steps \cite{wang2025vidorag, sun2025docagent}, and visually heterogeneous documents whose text, tables, and figures benefit from tool-specific inspection \cite{han2025mdocagent, zhu2025doclens}. However, adaptability in compute is difficult to bound before inference, and action policies and selection criteria remain sensitive to prompt phrasing, with silent failure when the controller commits early to wrong evidence. Moreover, reproducibility is complicated by stochastic generation and the absence of a single attributable forward pass, and the system inherits the recall ceiling of every retriever, parser, or OCR module it calls.

\section{Multi-Page Multimodal Representation}
\label{sec:modality}
Compared with single-page VRDU, the multi-page setting introduces a shared pressure on every modality. Full documents frequently exceed the backbone's context window, and even when they fit, the answer occupies only a small fraction of a long input within which attention is diluted and key evidence is readily lost in the middle of the context~\cite{liu2024lost}. Each modality must therefore trade page coverage against reading fidelity. The single-page mechanisms inherited from prior work, including OCR-text injection, per-image patch encoding, and per-token layout signals, are reviewed in earlier surveys~\cite{ding2025survey}. This section concentrates on the multi-page losses that text, visual, and structural evidence introduce (§\ref{sec:modality-text}, §\ref{sec:modality-visual}, §\ref{sec:modality-structure}), with modality fusion treated as a cross-cutting issue in Appendix~\ref{app:fusion}. Retrieval and navigation mechanisms recur across all three modality treatments and are examined in greater detail in §\ref{sec:inference-time} and §\ref{sec:training}.

% multi-page settings introduce distinct challenges for text, vision, and document structure: text may exceed the backbone context window and appear sparsely across pages; visual processing becomes expensive as both page number and resolution increase; and document-level structures such as section hierarchies, multi-page tables, and cross-references cannot be captured by single-page layout signals alone. We therefore discuss text, visual, and structural modelling in turn (§\ref{sec:modality-text}, §\ref{sec:modality-visual}, §\ref{sec:modality-structure}), focusing on the issues specific to MP-VRDU rather than those already covered in single-page surveys~\cite{ding2025survey}. Modality fusion is treated as a complementary cross-cutting issue in Appendix~\ref{app:fusion}.

\subsection{Text Modality}
\label{sec:modality-text}

% In SP-VRDU, text is typically processed by inserting OCR-extracted strings into the LLM prompt, encoding text jointly with layout and visual features through an auxiliary multimodal encoder, or training the model to read text directly from page pixels~\cite{ding2025survey}. Because a page fits entirely within the backbone's input, the model reads it once and locates the answer within the same pass. MP-VRDU faces two challenges: the document often exceeds the backbone's context window, and even when the full document fits, the answer occupies only a small fraction of a long input, where attention is diluted and key statements are easily lost in the middle~\cite{liu2024lost}.

Text is the most compact carrier of document semantics, yet in MP-VRDU its density becomes the central bottleneck. Tens of pages of extracted text must be reduced to the few sentences needed for an answer, and three problems shape how that reduction is performed. \textbf{Compression} can be applied at three points in the pipeline. Acquisition-stage approaches rely on OCR or PDF parsing~\cite{wang2024docllm} but flatten reading order, tables, and visual context before the model sees the document. Encoding-stage approaches retain all pages through page summaries, memory tokens, or long-context encoders~\cite{borchmann2025arctic}, fixing the compression before the question is seen. Retrieval-stage approaches index chunks or sampled evidence and pass only selected text to the reader~\cite{xie2024wukong}, scaling well but losing whatever retrieval missed. \textbf{Continuity} is a second concern when paragraphs, tables, and lists span page breaks, or when section meaning depends on earlier headings or definitions. Recurrent memory models carry page context forward~\cite{dong2024multi} and structure-aware pipelines group chunks under recovered headings~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion}, but both depend on an upstream decision about what context to retain, and adjacent-page expansion dilutes the query signal if applied uncritically~\cite{qian2026accurate}. \textbf{Heterogeneity} cuts across both, since text density varies sharply within a single document. Fixed token budgets, fixed page summaries, and fixed top-$k$ retrieval therefore over-compress dense pages while wasting capacity on sparse ones, and current systems rarely allocate budget jointly by page density, answer type, and downstream uncertainty.

% Text is the most information-dense modality in MP-VRDU, carrying the bulk of a document's explicit semantic content in a compact, searchable form, obtained either through OCR or recovered directly from page pixels by an LVLM. Extracted text from OCR supports cheap indexing and retrieval over documents that exceed context windows, natural answer-passage attribution, and bridges to visual elements via captions and references. However, this comes at the cost of OCR error propagation, fragile multi-column and table reading order, and the loss of chart, layout, and handwriting semantics. Page-to-document transformers typically rely on this acquisition method, treating OCR-extracted text as the primary per-page input, encoded jointly with layout and visual embeddings and exchanged across pages through document-level aggregation \cite{tito2023hierarchical, blau2024gram, borchmann2025arctic}. In contrast, pixel-recovered text avoids OCR dependence but demands high-resolution input and extensive text-recognition pretraining, both of which scale poorly with document length. Backbone-centric MLLMs typically rely on this method, reading text through the backbone's internal vision capabilities, applied to whole-document context in long-context variants \cite{hu2025mplug2, yu2024texthawk2} and selectively to retrieved regions in others \cite{zheng2026docvstar, jain2025simpledoc}. Retriever-generators use text as a searchable index over chunks, pages, or summaries and as evidence passed to the generator alongside or in place of visual inputs \cite{zhang2024cream, shin2025multidocfusion}, while agentic pipelines additionally use it as a navigation layer where outlines and section searches direct later visual inspection \cite{han2025mdocagent, sun2025docagent, wang2025vidorag}.

\subsection{Visual Modality}
\label{sec:modality-visual}
% In SP-VRDU, visual content is typically encoded by feeding image patches through a pretrained vision encoder, optionally at multiple resolutions or with shape-adaptive cropping, and aligned with text and layout features through projectors or adaptors~\cite{ding2025survey}. A single page can be encoded at the resolution required to read fine text, table cells, and chart elements within the model's input. MP-VRDU faces a sharper tension: visual encoding must remain fine-grained enough to recover charts, tables, and handwritten content that OCR cannot, yet scalable enough to apply across documents spanning tens or hundreds of pages. 

Visual evidence preserves signals that text extraction loses, but its cost scales with page count multiplied by resolution, and a page image simultaneously contains text-bearing pixels, non-textual evidence, and layout cues that compete for the same token budget. \textbf{Resolution versus coverage} is the first trade-off. Full-document high-resolution encoding is infeasible at realistic document length, so systems either compress every page through token-reduction modules~\cite{hu2025mplug2,yu2024texthawk2} or adaptive sub-image budgets~\cite{jia2024leopard}, or inspect only a selected subset at high resolution~\cite{zhu2025doclens,yan2025docseeker}. The first family preserves coverage but leaves fine-grained content vulnerable to downsampling, while the second inherits the recall ceiling of the selector. \textbf{Multi-signal competition} adds a second pressure, since the same visual budget must support text recovery, chart and figure understanding, handwriting, and spatial layout. Visual RAG systems encode rendered pages directly for retrieval~\cite{cho2024m3docrag,tanaka2025vdocrag}, avoiding parser loss but often treating the page as a single evidence unit, whereas element-level approaches crop tables, figures, and charts for finer inspection~\cite{zhu2025doclens} at the cost of detector dependence and possible separation between an element and its surrounding explanatory text. \textbf{Heterogeneity} compounds both, since visual complexity varies sharply across pages within a single document. Evidence-guided resolution allocation and adaptive image budgeting reduce this mismatch~\cite{yan2025docseeker,jia2024leopard}, but current allocation is still mostly local to pages or image counts and does not jointly decide whether the answer needs text recognition, figure semantics, or layout continuity.

% Visual elements in MP-VRDU range from text-bearing structures such as dense paargraphs and tables to non-textual evidence such as charts, figures, and handwriting that OCR cannot recover. Visual token cost scales with both page count and per-page resolution, making full document high-resolution encoding infeasible at realistic document length. This forces every architecture to trade page coverage against reading fidelity within a fixed token budget. Some models \cite{hu2025mplug2, zheng2026docvstar, jain2025simpledoc} keep all pages available at low resolution to preserve document-scale visual evidence at low token cost while relying on OCR or parsed text to supply finer reading where the down-sampled image is insufficient. Others \cite{hu2025mplug2, guo2026end} sidestep this tradeoff through selective high-resolution reading, restricting expensive visual encoding to pages or regions surfaced by retrieval, navigation, or controller driven inspection. Retriever-generator systems \cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag, li2025avir} take a different route, often use visual page embeddings purely as a retrieval signal rather than as a reading input. These approaches are not mutually exclusive, and many systems \cite{zheng2026docvstar, jain2025simpledoc} pair low-resolution thumbnails for navigation with selective high-resolution inspection of pages a retriever or tool-call surfaces. Each strategy, however, carries a characteristic failure mode. Low-resolution context loses fine text and small visual elements, selective high-resolution reading inherits the recall ceiling of whichever tool chooses what to read, and visual retrieval can rank pages whose layout looks relevant without confirming the answer-bearing region is present. Compression and tokenisation choices further determine how reliably embedded text, table cells, and figure annotations survive into the model input.

\subsection{Structure Modality}

\label{sec:modality-structure}

Structure turns isolated page observations into document evidence, and in MP-VRDU the difficult structure is not token geometry within one page but relationships whose scope crosses pages, namely section hierarchies, reading order, references, and continuations that bounding boxes cannot encode directly. \textbf{Hierarchy and reading order} is the foundational problem. Documents organise evidence through sections, subsections, lists, columns, and captions that may begin on one page and continue on another. Explicit parsers expose this structure as outlines, trees, or hierarchical indices~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion,sun2025docagent}, while some backbone-centric models attempt to recover order implicitly from pixels~\cite{yu2024texthawk2}. The unresolved gap is robustness, since parser errors propagate downstream while implicit recovery depends on high-resolution input that may already have been compressed. \textbf{Relations and references} demand following typed links rather than retrieving a single similar page. Graph and map-based methods make such relations first-class objects through knowledge graphs, page graphs, or document maps~\cite{wang2024knowledge,wu2025molorag,fu2026dmap}, improving navigability at the cost of edge quality bounded by extraction heuristics or LLM judgement. \textbf{Continuation} is the structural form of the text-continuity problem, since multi-page tables and section-spanning arguments require units larger than a page but smaller than the whole document. Hierarchical and cross-page retrieval preserve parent context~\cite{gong2025mhier,qian2026accurate}, but the boundary between useful context and irrelevant neighbours remains hard to set. Across all three modalities, the dominant failure mode is upstream loss, since compression, parsing, and retrieval decide what survives before reasoning begins; no modality currently supports query-conditional reading, and failures tend to be silent. The modalities are also coupled, since visual compression bounds pixel-recovered text and parser quality propagates into cross-reference resolution.

% In SP-VRDU, structural information is mainly captured as within-page layout, including 2D positional encodings, bounding-box coordinates, and reading-order signals attached to individual tokens. It is incorporated through layout-aware pretraining objectives, layout-as-prompt formulations, or implicit recovery from page pixels by an OCR-free MLLM. These mechanisms remain valid in MP-VRDU but only describe the geometry within each page. The structural problem distinctive to MP-VRDU is \textit{cross-page} structure: section hierarchies that span multiple pages, information referenced or continued across page boundaries, and document-level relationships such as figure-to-text or table-to-caption linkages that no longer fit within a single page's view. Per-token bounding boxes do not encode such relationships, and large-scale supervision for document-scale structure is expensive to obtain. Some approaches address this by training the backbone to predict cross-page structure end-to-end through multi-page parsing or document-level pretraining objectives~\cite{hu2025mplug2, duan2025docopilot}, but pretraining data at this scale remains scarce. 

% The structural problem distinctive to MP-VRDU is \textit{cross-page} structure such as section hierarchies that span multiple pages, information referenced or continued across page boundaries, and figure-to-text or table-to-caption linkages that no longer fit within a single page's view, none of which per-token bounding boxes can encode. The dominant route in current MP-VRDU is therefore to expose parsed structural information to the model directly. Three patterns have emerged. \textbf{Structure-aware chunking} uses a parser to extract section trees and heading hierarchies, then segments documents along semantic boundaries so retrieved units respect section scope rather than sliding-window cuts, as in MultiDocFusion~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion}. \textbf{Hierarchical retrieval indices} route queries first to coarse units such as sections or chapters and then to fine-grained chunks within the selected branch, reducing irrelevant pages while preserving cross-page context, as in MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} and MLDocRAG~\cite{zhang2026mldocrag}. \textbf{Graph-based traversal} converts headings, entities, and cross-references into a knowledge graph, allowing controllers to follow typed edges across pages rather than relying on dense similarity alone, as in KGP~\cite{wang2024knowledge}. All three depend on parser quality: errors in heading detection, table boundary segmentation, or reading-order extraction propagate directly into every downstream retrieval and navigation step.

% Layout in MP-VRDU spans two levels. Within a page, it captures reading order, columns, table structure, and bounding-box geometry, largely shared with single-page VRDU and covered in prior surveys \cite{ding2025survey}. Across pages, it captures document-level structure such as section hierarchy, page order, multi-page tables, and cross-references, which is the distinctive multi-page problem. Within-page layout is largely handled by mechanisms inherited from single-page VRDU, either through layout-aware pretraining objectives \cite{tito2023hierarchical, hu2025mplug2, zhu2025laytokenllm} or through implicit recovery from page pixels by an OCR-free MLLM \cite{yu2024texthawk2}. Cross-page layout is rarely captured by these mechanisms, as per-token spatial annotations describe individual tokens rather than relationships between sections, pages, or references, and pretraining supervision for document-scale structure is expensive to obtain. A growing line of work therefore exposes parsed structural information directly to the model. Section trees and heading hierarchies are extracted by a structural parser and used to chunk documents along semantic boundaries before retrieval, so that retrieved units respect section scope rather than sliding-window cuts \cite{shin2025multidocfusion}. Hierarchical retrieval indices route queries first to coarse levels such as sections or chapters and then to fine-grained units within the selected branch, reducing irrelevant pages and preserving cross-page context \cite{gong2025mhier, zhang2026mldocrag}. Knowledge graphs constructed over headings, entities, and cross-references convert layout into a navigable structure, allowing controllers to traverse from a starting page to related pages through typed edges rather than dense similarity alone \cite{wang2024knowledge}. Each route carries a distinct cost. Layout-as-objective demands pretraining supervision that is expensive at multi-page scale, implicit visual layout depends on backbone capability and high-resolution input, and explicit structure depends on parser quality, with errors in heading detection, table boundaries, or reading-order extraction propagating into every downstream retrieval and navigation step.

\section{Inference-Time Adaptation}
\label{sec:inference-time}
Inference-time adaptation refers to strategies that improve MP-VRDU performance through changes to the inference pipeline rather than to model parameters. A frozen LLM/MLLM backbone is paired with off-the-shelf retrievers, structural parsers, prompted reasoning loops, or multi-agent orchestrations, none of which require gradient updates on the backbone itself. This makes inference-time adaptation particularly suited to MP-VRDU settings where supervised data is scarce and expensive to train with, where backbones must remain exchangeable across systems, or where rapid integration with new document types is needed without retraining. We organise these strategies along the inference pipeline: evidence selection through retrieval and navigation (§\ref{sec:tf-retrieval}), inference-time reasoning strategies (§\ref{sec:tf-reasoning}), and coordination across multiple frozen components (§\ref{sec:agenic-orchestration}).

% \section{Inference-time adaptation}
% Training-free strategies improve MP-VRDU performance without updating the parameters of the underlying LLM and VLM backbones. Strategies include offline document-side preparation, such as parsing the document into a graph, tree, or outline, as well as online inference-side orchestrations through prompting, tool use, retrieval refinement, and multi-agent coordination. Compared to supervised training, retrieval tuning, and reinforcement learning, training-free strategies remain less systematically explored despite their growing prevalence in agentic and retrieval-based systems for multi-page document understanding.

\subsection{Retrieval and Navigation}
\label{sec:tf-retrieval}
Inference-time retrieval selects a compact subset of pages, chunks, or graph nodes for the reader using off-the-shelf encoders, structural parsers, or LLM-based rerankers, without fine-tuning the retriever on document data. Retrieval quality bounds every downstream reasoning step, so the choice of signal dominates system behaviour on long documents. Two broad families have emerged, distinguished by whether passages are scored in isolation or navigated through explicit relations.

\paragraph{Similarity-based Retrieval.}
Similarity-based methods score each passage against the query independently, using sparse lexical signals, dense embeddings, or combinations of both. The dominant dense signal in MP-VRDU is vision-language late-interaction encoding (ColPali, ColBERT-v2, ColQwen), which produces token-level query vectors and patch-level page embeddings and scores their maximum alignment, preserving fine-grained alignment between query terms and specific page regions. M3DocRAG~\cite{cho2024m3docrag} retrieves at chunk and page granularity with this signal alone; AVIR~\cite{li2025avir} adds an adaptive selector that adjusts the number of returned pages per query. Joint variants combine signals at the reranking stage, where CREAM~\cite{zhang2024cream} pairs BGE dense retrieval with iterative LLM-based reranking, SimpleDoc~\cite{jain2025simpledoc} combines ColPali embeddings with LLM-generated summary reranking under persistent working memory, and MDocAgent~\cite{han2025mdocagent} runs parallel text and image retrieval tracks whose outputs are aggregated downstream. Independent scoring handles paraphrase and cross-modal queries well, but cannot follow cross-page references or multi-hop dependencies that no single passage carries.

\paragraph{Structure-based Retrieval.}
Structure-based methods exploit a parsed representation of the document to make passage relations first-class objects. Tree-based variants such as MultiDocFusion~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion} and DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} extract section hierarchies, outlines and group passages along semantic boundaries so retrieved chunks preserve parent context, while MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} extends this by routing queries through dual in-page and cross-page indices. Graph-based variants treat headings, entities, and cross-references as graph nodes with typed edges, allowing controllers to traverse from one passage to related ones rather than relying on similarity alone: KGP~\cite{wang2024knowledge} uses an LLM as a traverser over a passage-and-structure graph, while MoLoRAG~\cite{wu2025molorag} and MLDocRAG~\cite{zhang2026mldocrag} extend this with logical relevance scoring or query-conditioned graph construction. Structure-based methods recover references that similarity scoring misses, at the cost of dependence on parser quality and traversal cost that grows with the hop count.

% \paragraph{Sparse Hierarchy-Aware Search.}
% Many models parse the input document into an explicit textual structure and perform sparse retrieval or traversal through term or structure anchored matching. MultiDocFusion~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion} uses a hierarchy parser to recover document, section, and subsection trees from scanned PDFs and groups passages along DFS-ordered Markdown headers, so retrieved chunks preserve their parent context (parser tuned). DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} instead extracts a tree-formatted XML outline from headings, sections, and captions and exposes it through section-level search and section-content fetch tools that navigate the parsed hierarchy directly. MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} extends the structure to a dual hierarchy of in-page flattened chunks and cross-page topological chunks, with LLM-based re-ranking across both indices, addressing the cross-page fragmentation that single-tree designs leave unresolved. However, sparse signals match on lexical or structural overlap and miss semantic connections and paraphrased queries, resulting in degraded performance on questions whose phrasing diverges from the document's surface form.

% \paragraph{Dense Embedding Search.}
% Dense embedding retrieval addresses this by encoding pages or chunks into learned representations, so queries match on semantic similarity rather than lexical or structural overlap. Many models \cite{wu2025docreact, wu2025molorag, han2025mdocagent, jain2025simpledoc, wang2025vidorag} adopt vision-language late-interaction encoders such as ColPali, ColBERT, and ColQwen as their dense retriever. These encoders embed the query as a sequence of contextual token vectors and each page as a bag of patch embeddings produced by a vision-language transformer over the rendered page image, then score a query-page pair by taking the maximum cosine similarity of each query token over all page patches and summing the result. MaxSim aggregation preserves fine-grained alignment between specific query terms and specific page regions, which is critical in MP-VRDU as a question token may align with a single figure caption, table cell, or paragraph buried in a long document. M3DocRAG~\cite{cho2024m3docrag} instantiates this pattern by retrieving at chunk and page granularity with the rest of the pipeline frozen. AVIR~\cite{li2025avir} adds an adaptive page selector that examines the per-query score distribution and either thresholds at a fixed cutoff for short documents or applies K-means clustering with a top-K bound on longer ones, so the number of pages handed to the reasoner scales with the score distribution rather than being fixed (lightweight head trained). Such methods handle paraphrase and cross-modal queries well, yet remain structure-blind on their own, since a section that should be retrieved as a unit can be split across embedding chunks and cross-page references are not represented as first-class objects.

% \paragraph{Joint Multi-Signal Search.}
% The complementary strengths of sparse and dense signals can be combined at the retrieval stage rather than committing to a single channel. CREAM pairs BGE coarse retrieval with iterative LLM-based re-ranking across overlapping chunk groups, so dense semantic candidates are filtered by an LLM judgement that draws on the full passage text \cite{zhang2024cream}. SimpleDoc combines ColPali page embeddings with LLM-generated summary re-ranking under a working memory that persists across rounds, so successive iterations can refine the joint score \cite{jain2025simpledoc}. MDocAgent runs parallel text and image retrieval tracks whose candidates are aggregated by a downstream agent, joining textual and visual evidence rather than fusing modalities at encoding time \cite{han2025mdocagent}. Such combinations improve recall by spanning what any single signal misses, but still score each candidate passage in isolation, so retrieval cannot follow logical links, cross-references, or multi-hop dependencies that span passages.

% \paragraph{Graph-Based Traversal}
% Graph traversal addresses this by indexing the document as a graph and navigating it through edges, resulting in strong inter-passage. KGP~\cite{wang2024knowledge} constructs a knowledge graph over passages and document-structure nodes such as pages, paragraphs, and tables, with edges encoding lexical similarity and structural belonging, then uses an instruction-tuned LLM as a traverser that predicts the next evidence node from the visited path. MoLoRAG~\cite{wu2025molorag} instead builds a page graph in which edge candidates are filtered by embedding similarity, then scores logical relevance with a frozen Qwen2.5-VL prompt and combines that with semantic similarity for multi-hop traversal, so retrieval can follow logical dependencies that surface similarity misses. MLDocRAG~\cite{zhang2026mldocrag} constructs the graph per query, anchoring chunk nodes to LVLM-generated query nodes and expanding through KNN edges between queries, so the structure adapts to the question rather than being fixed at index time. Graph methods reach references that scoring methods cannot, but results in construction overhead, edge-quality dependence, and traversal cost that scales with hop count.

\subsection{Inference-Time Reasoning Strategies}
\label{sec:tf-reasoning}
Inference-time reasoning strategies govern how a frozen LLM accesses, updates, and reasons over evidence during decoding, without modifying model parameters. In MP-VRDU, these strategies can be broadly categorised by whether evidence acquisition remains static or becomes dynamically interactive during inference. \textbf{Closed-buffer reasoning} operates over a fixed evidence set assembled before generation begins, whereas \textbf{tool-interactive reasoning} allows the model to iteratively acquire additional evidence through retrieval or page-level actions during the reasoning process.

\paragraph{Closed-buffer Reasoning.}
Closed-buffer reasoning performs multi-step reasoning over a fixed evidence buffer assembled by a prior retrieval step or by a compressed document representation. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting~\cite{wei2022chain} is the canonical instantiation, eliciting intermediate reasoning steps that integrate textual, tabular, and visual evidence before producing the final answer. MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} applies structured CoT prompts over hierarchical retrieval outputs, while SimpleDoc~\cite{jain2025simpledoc} adds self-refinement and self-verification prompts that emit revised queries when the assembled evidence is judged insufficient. Closed-buffer reasoning is fundamentally bounded by retrieval completeness: evidence absent from the buffer cannot be recovered by subsequent reasoning or verification.

\paragraph{Tool-interactive Reasoning.}
Tool-interactive reasoning interleaves reasoning and evidence acquisition during inference, allowing newly observed pages to guide subsequent reasoning steps. ReAct prompting~\cite{yao2022react} is the canonical instantiation, alternating intermediate reasoning traces with tool calls whose outputs feed the next reasoning stage. Doc-React~\cite{wu2025docreact} instantiates this with flat retrieval, sub-query refinement, and entropy-based stopping, while outline-driven variants combine ReAct with the structured navigation discussed in §\ref{sec:tf-retrieval}. Adaptive evidence acquisition improves localisation and supports more exploratory multi-page reasoning, but introduces tool latency that grows with rounds, sensitivity to prompt design and to early action choices that subsequent steps cannot recover from, and attention dilution within fetched pages at their fixed reading resolution.

\subsection{Agentic Orchestration}
\label{sec:agenic-orchestration}
Retriever-generator and agentic pipelines often distribute a single MP-VRDU question across multiple specialised LLM or VLM calls, each handling a particular modality, reasoning step, or verification role. The frozen backbones are not modified; coordination is achieved through prompt design and call ordering. The two families correspond directly to whether the trajectory of calls is fixed at design time or adapts to the question at inference: retriever-generator pipelines run a trajectory fixed in advance regardless of how many agents they invoke, while agentic pipelines treat the call sequence itself as a variable.

\paragraph{Fixed-Trajectory Pipelines.}
Fixed-trajectory pipelines follow a control flow set in advance: which calls are made, in what order, and how their outputs are combined are all determined at design time. Modality-partitioned variants dispatch readers by input type and aggregate their outputs through a final synthesis step: MDocAgent~\cite{han2025mdocagent} runs five parallel agents fused by a summariser, while M2RAG~\cite{duan2025m2rag} splits work between a text filter, a visual extractor, and a modal fuser that falls back to one modality when the other reports no information. Such pipelines improve coverage relative to single-agent baselines, but a wrong choice of routing or ordering cannot be revised mid-execution.

\paragraph{Adaptive-Trajectory Pipelines.}
Adaptive-trajectory pipelines treat the call sequence itself as a variable: which agents are invoked and how many rounds the loop runs are decided per question rather than fixed in advance. Loop based variants such as ViDoRAG~\cite{wang2025vidorag} cycle a seeker that retrieves coarse candidates, an inspector that reviews them at fine resolution, and an answerer that performs a consistency check, with a Gaussian mixture model setting per-query K bounds from the similarity distribution rather than using a static top-K. Proposer-verifier variants insert an explicit verification stage between candidate generation and final answer: DocLens~\cite{zhu2025doclens} samples multiple candidates and routes them through an adjudicator that compares reasoning paths, while DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} pairs an actor with a reviewer that cross-checks through complementary tools and writes disagreements into a shared reflection memory. Adaptive-trajectory pipelines improve the accuracy-latency trade-off, but inference cost remains hard to bound before execution, scaling jointly with the number of calls involved.

% \paragraph{Modality-Specialised Pipelines.}
% Some models specialise agents by input modality or by stage of the evidence pipeline and aggregates their outputs through a final synthesis. MDocAgent~\cite{han2025mdocagent} runs a five-agent pipeline in which a general agent provides initial multimodal context, a critical agent extracts key evidence from both modalities, dedicated text and image agents read their respective tracks in parallel, and a summariser fuses the four outputs into a single answer. M2RAG~\cite{duan2025m2rag} splits work between a text filter, a visual extractor, and a modal fuser that applies a confidence-conditional rule, falling back to one modality when the other reports no information and fusing only when both contribute (LoRA trained). Parallel pipelines improve modality coverage compared to single-track retrieval, but lack an error-correction stage, so an incorrect output from one branch can dominate aggregation when the other branch lacks counter-evidence.

% \paragraph{Verification and Adjudication.}
% Other models addresses this challenge by inserting an explicit verifier between proposal and answer. DocLens~\cite{zhu2025doclens} samples multiple candidate answers from a reasoning module and routes them through an adjudicator that compares reasoning paths and selects the most consistent conclusion, so candidates are judged against each other rather than fused unconditionally. DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} pairs an actor with a reviewer that cross-checks through complementary tools, with low tool-overlap reported between the two agents, and writes any disagreement into a shared reflection memory that updates the actor's behaviour on subsequent rounds. Adjudication catches single-branch errors that fusion cannot, but the verifier draws on the same backbone family as the actor, so shared blind spots persist and the verifier can over-reject correct candidates under noisy tool output.

% \paragraph{Adaptive Orchestration.}
% A third family treats the loop itself as adaptive, allocating compute per question and per role rather than running a fixed pipeline. ViDoRAG~\cite{wang2025vidorag} cycles a seeker that retrieves coarse candidates, an inspector that reviews them at fine resolution and emits reflective feedback, and an answerer that performs a consistency check, with a Gaussian mixture model setting per-query K bounds for each modality from the similarity distribution rather than using a static top-K. MACT~\cite{yu2025mact} decomposes the loop further into planning, execution, judgement, and answer agents, separating the judge from the corrector to avoid the same agent excusing its own errors, and applies agent-wise adaptive test-time scaling so compute is allocated per role and per question (RL-trained). Adaptive orchestration improves the accuracy-latency trade-off but results in latency that scales jointly with the number of rounds and agents, a limitation shared across all multi-agent designs.

\section{Training Strategies}
\label{sec:training}
While §\ref{sec:inference-time} held the backbone fixed, many MP-VRDU systems extend these pipelines by training specific components. This section instead organises MP-VRDU training by the inference-time component being trained, namely the retriever (§\ref{sec:train-retrieval}), the reasoner (§\ref{sec:train-reasoning}), and the agentic orchestration policy (§\ref{sec:train-orchestration}). Two cross-cutting techniques, multi-stage curriculum training (§\ref{sec:train-curriculum}) and parameter-efficient tuning (§\ref{sec:train-peft}), recur across all three targets.

% Training strategies in MP-VRDU are closely tied to how each architecture controls the cost and complexity of multi-page document understanding. While the previous section describe how models represent documents and combine text, visual, and layout evidence, this section focuses on how these capabilities are learned.

\subsection{Trained Retrieval Components}
\label{sec:train-retrieval}
Off-the-shelf encoders such as ColPali and ColQwen~\cite{Faysse2024-wq} score pages in isolation, leaving three gaps that retriever training aims to address. Modality-alignment variants pretrain a visual document encoder against OCR text: VDocRAG~\cite{tanaka2025vdocrag} uses RCR and RCG objectives to compress page images into single-vector embeddings that retain text alignment without late-interaction cost. Cross-page-fusion variants freeze the encoder and learn a neighbour-blending head: DFVC~\cite{qian2026accurate} adds an MLP adapter with gating and residual connections that fuses each page embedding with its neighbours so evidence spread across pages becomes retrievable. Logical-relevance variants train a small scorer over a page graph: MoLoRAG~\cite{wu2025molorag} fine-tunes a Qwen2.5-VL scorer that ranks neighbour pages during graph traversal, supplying the cross-page logical signal embedding similarity alone often misses.

% Beyond architectural design, another key challenge lies in effective retrieval mechanisms. Rather than encoding entire documents, many approaches adopt retrieval-augmented pipelines, where models first select relevant pages or segments before performing reasoning. In this setting, the performance of the system is heavily dependent on the quality of the retriever. For example, VDocRAG~\cite{tanaka2025vdocrag} introduces self-supervised representation compression retrieval that aligns visual document representations with OCR-derived textual content, enabling large vision-language models to learn document embeddings directly from unlabeled document-image pairs. 

\subsection{Trained Reasoning Components} % For Agentic/R-G reasoning
\label{sec:train-reasoning}
Prompted reasoning leaves quality sensitive to prompt design and base-model behaviour. Reward-driven variants train a reasoning policy with explicit evidence supervision: DocR1~\cite{xiong2026docr1} fine-tunes Qwen2.5-VL with Evidence-Page-Guided GRPO, whose reward combines format compliance, answer accuracy, and a page-citation indicator, producing structured \texttt{<think>}, \texttt{<evidence\_page>}, and \texttt{<answer>} outputs. Trace-imitation variants instil a reasoning chain through SFT and preference optimisation: CoR~\cite{guo2026end} bakes in a four-stage plan-execute-integrate-verify chain through LoRA SFT on a mixed corpus, full-parameter SFT on the 26K CoR-Dataset and Mask-AR, then DPO on 5K preference pairs. Uncertainty-driven variants train an aggregation head rather than a trajectory: DocSLM~\cite{hannan2025docslm} learns an entropy-based calibrator that selects the lowest-uncertainty answer across streaming segments, providing a self-consistency signal under tight parameter budgets.

% As document understanding tasks become increasingly complex, recent work has gradually moved toward more agentic reasoning frameworks, especially reinforcement learning (RL). Agentic methods iteratively explore document pages, refine intermediate reasoning steps, and dynamically select evidence. DocR1~\cite{} proposes Evidence Page-Guided GRPO (EviGRPO), which encourages a coarse-to-fine reasoning strategy where the model first identifies relevant evidence pages before generating answers.

\subsection{Trained Agentic Orchestration}
\label{sec:train-orchestration}
Prompt-only orchestration is unreliable on smaller open-weight backbones that cannot follow ReAct protocols. Behavioural-cloning variants distil agent trajectories from a stronger model: DocDancer~\cite{zhang2026docdancer} fine-tunes a 4B or 30B Qwen3 agent on synthesised exploration-then-synthesis traces that pair ReAct \texttt{Search} and \texttt{Read} actions with intent-conditioned tool selection. Reinforcement-learned variants reward correct tool use and trajectory shape: Doc-V*~\cite{zheng2026docvstar} couples SFT on coarse-to-fine ReAct traces with GRPO over a two-action space of \texttt{retrieval\_page} and \texttt{fetch\_page} calls plus entropy-based stopping, while MACT~\cite{yu2025mact} trains a multi-agent policy with agent-wise adaptive test-time scaling that allocates sample budgets per role of planner, executor, judge, and answerer by per-question difficulty.

\subsection{Parameter Efficient Tuning}
\label{sec:train-peft}
Full backbone fine-tuning is prohibitive at long-document context lengths and erases pretrained capabilities, so the dominant MP-VRDU pattern freezes the backbone and trains lightweight modules such as LoRA matrices, projection heads, or task-specific adapters. LayTokenLLM~\cite{zhu2025laytokenllm} freezes the LLM and adds a layout tokenisation module plus LoRA, encoding spatial layout through compressed positional tokens with negligible context overhead, while CREAM~\cite{zhang2024cream} applies the LLaMA-Adapter-V2 recipe to a LLaMA2-7B answerer, training only LoRA matrices, attention pooling, and bias and normalisation parameters above a frozen Pix2Struct encoder. The same pattern recurs in DFVC's frozen-ColPali adapter (§\ref{sec:train-retrieval}) and DocDancer's LoRA trajectory tuning (§\ref{sec:train-orchestration}), confirming that PEFT versus full fine-tuning is more often a compute-budget decision than a target-component one.

% A common strategy is to reuse strong pretrained LLMs or LVLMs and update only a small number of task-specific components. Rather than training vision encoders or language backbones end-to-end, these methods freeze most pretrained parameters and introduce lightweight modules such as LoRA, projectors, adapters, layout tokenizers, or task-specific heads. 
% LayTokenLLM~\cite{zhu2025laytokenllm} exemplifies this by keeping the LLM backbone frozen and introducing a lightweight layout tokenization module together with LoRA-based adaptation. Rather than modifying the architecture, it injects layout information into the input sequence using a compressed representation and a specialized positional encoding scheme that avoids additional context overhead, enabling efficient scaling to long multi-page documents. As an alternative, some work adopts retrieval-augmented strategies, which avoid directly modeling entire documents by selecting only the most relevant content for downstream reasoning. CREAM~\cite{zhang2024cream} introduces a coarse-to-fine retrieval framework. It first retrieves candidate text chunks based on embedding similarity, and then applies multi-round LLM-based re-ranking to identify the most relevant evidence. Extending this idea, MoLoRAG~\cite{wu2025molorag} incorporates logical reasoning into retrieval by constructing a graph over document pages and performing multi-hop traversal, enabling the model to capture both semantic and logical relevance across pages.

\subsection{Curriculum / Multi-Stage Training}
\label{sec:train-curriculum}
Several systems converge on a multi-stage curriculum that starts on single-page or local objectives before introducing document-scale supervision, exploiting the fact that text reading and layout understanding transfer from single-page data while cross-page integration must be learned separately. mPLUG-DocOwl2~\cite{hu2025mplug2} uses three stages of single-image structure learning on DocStruct4M, MP-DocStruct1M continue-pretraining, and multi-task fine-tuning, while DocSLM~\cite{hannan2025docslm} adds two more stages for a five-stage schedule covering image-OCR alignment, image- and document-level compression, instruction tuning, and streaming-abstention calibration.

It first performs single-image structure learning to ensure that compressed visual tokens preserve document text and layout information, then pretrain on multi-page parsing.

\section{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}
Datasets in MP-VRDU span three roles: pretraining (PT), fine-tuning (FT), and benchmarking (BM), with several datasets occupying more than one role across different systems. Per-dataset statistics are available in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_survey} and per-paper adoption in Table~\ref{tab:datasets_used}.

\subsection{Pretraining}
\label{sec:data-pt}
Pretraining at scale is confined to the page-to-document and backbone-centric families, since retriever-generator and agentic pipelines inherit pretrained vision-language backbones and skip the stage entirely. The signals that pretraining targets have shifted accordingly. Early work relied on single-page OCR archives such as IIT-CDIP~\cite{lewis2006iitcdip} and OCR-IDL~\cite{biten2022ocridl}, which supplied text-recognition supervision but no cross-page structure. Later corpora couple parsing objectives with multi-page document inputs to teach token-position alignment and structural awareness directly: DocStruct4M~\cite{hu2024mplug} mixes single- and multi-page samples with structure-aware parsing supervision, and the purely multi-page MP-DocStruct1M, Doc-750K, and bilingual scientific PaperPDF corpora~\cite{hu2025mplug2,duan2025docopilot,xie2024wukong} now support multi-image continued pretraining over genuine document inputs. The text-recognition objective that defined the single-page era has receded as a primary pretraining target in favour of structure-aware and document-scale supervision.

\subsection{Fine-tuning}
\label{sec:data-ft}
Per-page human annotation does not scale to the page counts typical of MP-VRDU, so fine-tuning has converged on two strategies, often combined within a single system. The first aggregates publicly released question-answer collections into broad instruction mixes that exercise heterogeneous question and answer types~\cite{tanaka2024instructdoc,jia2024leopard,hu2025mplug2,duan2025docopilot}. The second curates a bespoke corpus for the system under study, typically synthesised by a strong vision-language model writing question-answer pairs, reasoning traces, or agent trajectories over scraped PDFs~\cite{xie2024wukong,tanaka2025vdocrag,wu2025molorag,zhang2026docdancer,guo2026end}. Synthetic generation has become the dominant scaling path for fine-tuning data, and the underlying PDF pools are typically drawn from the same scientific archives, slide repositories, and benchmark document sources that also supply evaluation. Layered above supervised fine-tuning, a growing body of work applies reinforcement or preference learning with shaped rewards that credit correct evidence-page citation, faithful chain-of-thought structure, or appropriate tool use rather than final-answer accuracy alone~\cite{xiong2026docr1,guo2026endtoend,yan2025docseeker,lin2026mmdocr1,xu2026mmr5,wang2026vragrl}. The supervision pools at this stage remain small relative to the supervised mixes that precede them, indicating that reinforcement signals are used for behaviour shaping---instilling evidence grounding, output formatting, and multi-step reasoning patterns---rather than for further scaling.

\subsection{Benchmarking}
\label{sec:data-bm}
Benchmarks have evolved through several tiers that have accumulated rather than replaced one another. Single-page extractive evaluation on DocVQA, InfographicVQA, and ChartQA~\cite{mathew2021docvqa,mathew2022infographicvqa,masry2022chartqa} persists as a per-page baseline, with mid-length multi-page corpora such as MP-DocVQA, DUDE, and SlideVQA~\cite{tito2023hierarchical,van2023dude,tanaka2023slidevqa} extending the same extractive answer formats into a few-page setting. Long-document multimodal benchmarks then introduced documents whose total length routinely exceeds backbone context windows and whose evidence requires cross-page integration over mixed text, tables, charts, and figures~\cite{ma2024mmlongbench,deng2025longdocurl,zou2024docbench,dong2025mmdocir,chia2025m}, and retrieval-oriented benchmarks now score evidence-page selection and layout-level localisation alongside answer accuracy~\cite{hui2024uda,xiong2026docr1,zhu2025doclens}. The most recent additions push further into cross-document settings whose corpora exceed any single long document~\cite{cho2024m3docrag,wang2025vidorag,tanaka2025vdocrag,suri2025visdom} and into domains beyond the academic, financial, and slide-deck sources that have dominated earlier work~\cite{chen2025svrag}. Despite this proliferation, evaluation in practice has consolidated around a small set of long-document and mid-length benchmarks that recur in most reported comparisons and serve as the de-facto standards for cross-model assessment.

\section{Conclusions and Challenges}
\label{sec:challenges}
This survey reframes multi-page visually rich document understanding around cross-page evidence management, organising architectures, modalities, inference-time adaptation, and training strategies by how each handles evidence that no single forward pass can contain. A consistent pattern emerges across families: progress comes from moving evidence handling out of the backbone — through compression, retrieval, or iterative orchestration — but each shift relocates the bottleneck rather than removing it. Closing the remaining gaps in efficiency, faithfulness, evidence pipelines, supervision integrity, and evaluation scope is the open frontier for the next generation of MP-VRDU systems.
\paragraph{Efficiency and Deployability.}
\label{sec:chal-eff}
Agentic and retrieval-augmented pipelines routinely issue tens of LLM or MLLM calls per question, with token costs scaling jointly with document length and trajectory length. The surveyed literature reports almost no inference-cost analysis: end-to-end latency, token budgets per query, and accuracy–cost Pareto curves are largely absent, despite their centrality to enterprise deployment. Future work should treat compute as a first-class evaluation axis, develop efficient inference techniques specific to long documents such as cross-round caching, speculative decoding, and adaptive depth, and explore compact MP-VRDU models~\cite{hannan2025docslm} that operate under realistic on-device or privacy constraints.

\paragraph{Faithfulness and Abstention.}
\label{sec:chal-faith}
Long documents amplify hallucination risk: when retrieval misses or compression discards the answer-bearing region, current systems still produce confident answers. Few surveyed systems support abstention, calibrated uncertainty, or citation-grounded generation, leaving downstream users without signals to distinguish supported answers from fabrications. Future work should integrate evidence-grounded decoding, learned abstention against unanswerable or unretrievable questions, and trajectory-level faithfulness audits that verify the cited evidence supports the final answer.

\paragraph{Evidence Pipeline Bottleneck.}
\label{sec:chal-evidence}
MP-VRDU systems must reconcile high-resolution per-page reading with document-scale evidence aggregation, an objective that exceeds any MLLM's compute and context budget~\cite{ma2024mmlongbench}. Every architectural family takes a lossy compromise: compression silently drops fine-grained content and is typically fixed at index time so evidence is discarded before the question is seen; retrieval bounds answer accuracy from above while broadening the retrieved set introduces attention dilution; and iterative retrieval lifts this ceiling only partially, inheriting the recall of every tool it calls. Cross-page structure such as section hierarchies and inter-page references compounds this further, as none of the three current routes (§\ref{sec:modality-structure}) scales cleanly. Future work should explore query-conditional and modality-aware compression that retains evidence based on a question's evidential demands, retrieval that follows cross-references and logical relations rather than scoring passages in isolation, and learned cross-page structural representations that survive token compression.

\paragraph{Supervision Scarcity and Contamination.}
\label{sec:chal-sup}
Per-page annotation does not scale to documents averaging 8–20 pages, so most systems rely on LVLM-synthesised QA and trajectories over scraped PDFs~\cite{tanaka2024instructdoc, jain2025simpledoc, xiong2026docr1, zhang2026docdancer}. Nine of the twelve papers that introduce a synthesised corpus source their PDFs from existing evaluation benchmarks, and MP-DocVQA alone appears in 15 fine-tuning mixes and 17 evaluation suites of the 36 surveyed papers, with DUDE and DocVQA showing similar overlap. The result is a contamination loop in which a model trains on a strong LVLM's behaviour over benchmark PDFs and is then evaluated against those same PDFs, often under metrics judged by the same LVLM. No surveyed paper reports synthetic-data quality audits, document-hash de-duplication against test pools, or backbone-level contamination checks. Future work should adopt held-out closed-test benchmarks, contamination declarations covering pretraining, fine-tuning, and backbone leakage, and generator–evaluator separation when synthetic data is involved.

\paragraph{Evaluation Methodology and Scope.}
\label{sec:chal-eval}
Beyond contamination, two further evaluation gaps persist. Answer-only accuracy masks retrieval failure and rewards correct answers reached for the wrong reason; newer benchmarks score retrieval and page-localisation alongside answers~\cite{hui2024uda, xiong2026docr1, zhu2025doclens}, but the practice is not yet standardised, and agentic pipelines produce stochastic trajectories that complicate reproducibility. Task and language scope also remain narrow: surveyed systems are overwhelmingly DocVQA-centric and English-centric, while real-world MP-VRDU needs span summarisation, cross-document reasoning, and structured extraction across diverse non-English layouts and writing systems where reading order, script direction, and document conventions differ substantially. Future work should standardise trajectory-level evaluation paired with retrieval and grounding metrics, report stochasticity bounds for agentic systems, and broaden benchmark coverage across task types, document domains, and languages, particularly low-resource and non-Latin-script settings that current pretraining corpora barely touch.

\section*{Limitations}
This survey covers multi-page VRDU models published before May 2026 and is bounded by the rapid pace of the field, with new architectures, training-free methods, and benchmarks appearing during preparation that may not be reflected here. Selection prioritises representative coverage of architectural and inference-time strategies over exhaustive enumeration. Cross-model performance comparisons are inherently limited since training distributions, benchmark coverage, and evaluation metrics are bespoke per system, and quantitative claims should be interpreted as indicative rather than definitive.

\paragraph{Search keywords.}
Queries combined a multi-page cluster with a task or method cluster. \paragraph{Inclusion and exclusion criteria.}
A paper was retained if it (i) proposed a model, framework, system, or pipeline rather than a benchmark or dataset alone, and (ii) reported quantitative results on at least one multi-page benchmark with multi-page handling as an explicit design contribution. Excluded categories were single-page-only systems benchmarked solely on DocVQA, InfographicVQA, ChartQA, FUNSD, or CORD; survey, position, and opinion papers; generic OCR or table-extraction tools without VQA or reasoning evaluation; long-context LLM benchmarks where documents are one input type among many; and dataset-only contributions, which are catalogued separately in Appendix~\ref{dataset_detailed}. Peer-reviewed venue publications were preferred, while arXiv preprints were retained only where the contribution introduces a clear architectural, training, or orchestration novelty.

\paragraph{Knowledge extraction and verification.}
Each retained paper was converted to markdown and processed through LLM-assisted key-information extraction with evidence-span location, populating per-model fields covering architectural family, OCR dependency, vision encoder, LLM backbone, adaptors and projectors, page-rendering resolution, training stages, datasets used per stage, and reported scores on each benchmark. Every extracted field was manually verified against the source paper before being committed to the corpus tables.

\paragraph{Dataset catalogue.}
The dataset catalogue in Appendix~\ref{app:datasets} was built primarily from the pretraining, fine-tuning, and benchmarking datasets named by every paper in the surveyed corpus, supplemented with a small set of datasets the corpus does not yet adopt but that cover MP-VRDU-relevant settings such as long scientific documents, multi-page tables, and cross-page reasoning suites, included to surface datasets whose adoption may strengthen future cross-system comparison.

\section{More Framework Details}
\label{sec:framework-details}

\subsection{Multimodal Fusion}
\label{app:fusion}
General MLLM-based VRDU surveys cover common fusion mechanisms such as neural fusion, target-oriented fusion, and prompt-based fusion \cite{ding2025survey}. The multi-page setting adds a further axis: at which point in the pipeline modalities are combined, and how selectively that combination is applied across pages. Full text-vision-layout fusion across an entire document is rarely computationally feasible, so the surveyed systems fall into three approximate stages of fusion, catalogued below.

\paragraph{Early Fusion.}
Text, visual, and layout features are combined before or during document encoding. In page-to-document transformers, fusion is local: OCR tokens, bounding boxes, visual patches, and question tokens are combined within each page and then aggregated through page summaries \cite{tito2023hierarchical}, document-level tokens \cite{blau2024gram}, recurrent memory \cite{dong2024multi}, or sparse text-image-layout attention \cite{borchmann2025arctic}. Backbone-centric MLLMs apply early fusion at projection time, mapping visual or multimodal features into the LLM token space through adapters, projectors, or document-former modules \cite{tanaka2024instructdoc, duan2025docopilot, jia2024leopard}. The pattern preserves fine-grained multimodal alignment at the cost of joint computation over many pages.

\paragraph{Late Fusion.}
Modalities are kept separate during retrieval or inspection and combined only after candidate evidence has been selected. Retriever-generator systems identify relevant passages via text chunks before visual generation \cite{zhang2024cream}, perform page-image retrieval that keeps multimodal evidence fused at the page level \cite{cho2024m3docrag}, or combine parsed text, visual descriptions, parent pages, and cross-page summaries only for retrieved evidence \cite{gong2025mhier}. Agentic pipelines extend the pattern by inspecting text, image, table, and page-level evidence separately before final synthesis or adjudication \cite{han2025mdocagent, duan2025m2rag, zhu2025doclens}. The pattern scales to long documents but depends on retrieval and routing quality.

\paragraph{Staged Fusion.}
Local alignment is combined with selective document-level reasoning. Modalities are fused within pages or regions, then compressed, retrieved, or stored selectively before a final cross-page fusion step. Some hierarchical and recurrent models approximate the pattern by producing page-level summaries or memories prior to document-level aggregation \cite{tito2023hierarchical, dong2024multi}, and compact long-document models reduce local multimodal evidence into fixed-size page representations \cite{hannan2025docslm}. Fully developed staged fusion is rare in the surveyed corpus.

\subsection{Model Survey by OCR Dependency}
\label{app:ocr-survey}
Table~\ref{tab:model_survey_ocr} reorganises the surveyed systems along the OCR-dependency axis to complement the architectural grouping in Table~\ref{tab:model_survey_arch}. OCR-using systems dominate page-to-document transformers, which require explicit text tokens as input, and remain common across retriever-generator pipelines that index parsed text alongside page images. OCR-free systems concentrate in backbone-centric MLLMs that rely on the backbone's internal vision capabilities, and in agentic pipelines orchestrating frozen LVLMs. Modality coverage tracks this split, since OCR-using systems typically expose text, vision, and layout while OCR-free systems usually operate over visual tokens alone. ``OCR Note'' column denotes hybrid cases in which OCR is incorporated as an auxiliary framework component or appears only during training. Read together with Table~\ref{tab:model_survey_arch}, we demonstrate that OCR dependency is largely orthogonal to architectural family rather than a defining feature, as discussed in \S\ref{sec:architecture}.

\subsection{Reported Performance by Architecture}
\label{app:performance}
Table~\ref{tab:model_performance_arch} summarises reported scores on the five most-adopted multi-page benchmarks, grouped by architectural family. The matrix is sparse, as most papers report on only two or three of the five benchmarks, and metric conventions diverge across MP-DocVQA (ANLS), DUDE (ANLS), LongDocURL (Accuracy), MMLongBench-Doc (Accuracy and F1), and SlideVQA (EM and F1). Page-to-document transformers concentrate on mid-length benchmarks where the dataset matches the architectural inductive bias, with limited coverage of long-document tasks. Backbone-centric MLLMs span a wider range, with newer entries such as DocR1 and Docopilot reporting on long-document benchmarks as well. Retriever-generator and agentic systems dominate the long-document frontier, since selective evidence access is the design lever that makes 50+ page evaluation tractable. Cross-family comparison should account for backbone scale and proprietary LLM dependency, as agentic pipelines often pair a closed-source generator with a retrieval module while page-to-document and backbone-centric families typically report numbers under open-source weights. The reported scores should be read alongside Table~\ref{tab:model_training_status}, which clarifies whether a number reflects supervised fine-tuning on the benchmark or zero-shot evaluation.

\subsection{General Domain MLLM for MP-VRDU} 
\label{app:general-MLLM}
Frontier general-domain multimodal LLMs are widely adopted for MP-VRDU and handle short multi-page inputs reasonably well when the document fits in context. The multi-page setting, however, introduces structural pressures that single-page VRDU did not surface: documents frequently exceed the backbone's context window, answer-bearing evidence is sparsely distributed across pages so attention dilutes over long inputs~\cite{liu2024lost}, and per-page reading fidelity competes directly with whole-document coverage under a fixed token budget. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:model_performance_arch}, even the strongest frontier general MLLMs trail specialised MP-VRDU systems by a wide margin on long-document benchmarks such as MMLongBench-Doc and LongDocURL, with smaller open-weight models degrading further still. These limitations motivate the specialised architectures examined in the rest of this paper, which decouple evidence selection from reading, distribute work across modality-specific components, or iteratively acquire evidence rather than relying on a single long-context forward pass.

\subsection{Training Status by Component}
\label{app:training-status}
Table~\ref{tab:model_training_status} records which components are trained, frozen, or not applicable in each surveyed system, broken down by vision encoder, LLM backbone, and adaptor across pretraining, instruction-tuning, and fine-tuning stages. Three patterns emerge. End-to-end systems train multiple components, often jointly, and account for most page-to-document encoding models and several backbone-centric adaptation models that absorb document evidence into the backbone. Partially trained systems freeze the heavy backbone and update only a light adaptor or projection layer, a pattern common to retriever-generator pipelines and to retrieval-augmented backbone variants, since parameter-efficient adaptation allows reuse of strong pretrained vision-language priors. Training-free systems perform no parameter updates at all, the dominant pattern in agentic pipelines that orchestrate frozen LVLMs through prompting, tool use, and multi-agent decomposition. The training-status view also clarifies why some numbers in Table~\ref{tab:model_performance_arch} are not directly comparable, as systems trained on a benchmark naturally outperform training-free systems evaluated zero-shot on the same suite. Empty rows for training-free entries reflect this orchestration-only design rather than missing data.

\subsection{Model Components Details}
\label{app:components}
Table~\ref{tab:model_components} lists the concrete per-model components of each surveyed system, organised by architectural family. Each row reports the vision backbone, the input resolution or page-rendering setting, the LLM backbone, and the adaptors or projectors that connect them. Where a model lists multiple backbones, slashes denote interchangeable choices reported in the paper, while commas separate components used together in distinct roles. The four families differ markedly in how they assemble these parts. Page-to-document transformers train smaller LLM backbones such as T5 variants or DocFormerv2 paired with custom layout-aware projectors and document tokens. Backbone-centric MLLMs reuse standard vision-language stacks such as Qwen2.5-VL, mPLUG-Owl2, and SigLIP variants, and add light projection or compression modules, often updating only LoRA adapters or compressors. Retriever-generator pipelines decouple a frozen retriever such as ColPali, ColQwen, or VisRAG from a frozen LVLM and train only the scorer, fuser, or reranker. Agentic pipelines mostly orchestrate frozen frontier VLMs through prompting and tool use, with parameter updates limited to a few systems that fine-tune their agent backbone via SFT or GRPO.

\subsection{Open-Source Code Releases}
\label{app:open-source}
Table~\ref{tab:model_open_source} lists the surveyed MP-VRDU models that publish their implementation through a public GitHub repository. Sixteen of the 36 papers link such a repository directly in their source PDF. A further three systems, DocLens, RAG-DocVQA, and VDocRAG, distribute through \texttt{github.io} project pages rather than full code repositories, and MACT states that source code will be released publicly without yet providing a URL. The first is OCR and layout self-supervision, used by Hi-VT5 \cite{tito2023hierarchical} on OCR-IDL \cite{biten2022ocridl} (200K pages), Arctic-TILT \cite{borchmann2025arctic} on CCpdf \cite{turski2023ccpdf}, and Texthawk2 \cite{yu2024texthawk2} on IIT-CDIP \cite{lewis2006iitcdip}. The second is document-structure pretraining over synthesised QA, supplying weakly-supervised text-to-position alignment through DocStruct4M (4M cross-domain samples) \cite{hu2024mplug}, the purely multi-page MP-DocStruct1M (1.1M samples) \cite{hu2025mplug2}, the scientific corpus PaperPDF (1.1M bilingual PDF/QA pairs) \cite{xie2024wukong}, and Doc-750K (758K QA / 3.1M images / 251K conversations) \cite{duan2025docopilot}. The third is generic VLM pretraining followed by document continue-pretraining, in which a web-scale vision-language corpus such as Cauldron \cite{laurenccon2024building} is combined with document-specific data, as in Leopard \cite{jia2024leopard} and Texthawk2 \cite{yu2024texthawk2}. The field is converging on this third recipe, with token-level structure prediction over synthesised QA displacing simple OCR-text masked-language-modelling as the dominant document pretext task.

% \subsection{Fine-Tuning and Instruction Datasets}
% Two strategies dominate fine-tuning. Task-mixture fine-tuning, used by mPLUG-DocOwl2 \cite{hu2025mplug2}, Leopard \cite{jia2024leopard}, Docopilot \cite{duan2025docopilot}, and DocR1 \cite{xiong2026docr1}, aggregates a broad pool of public sources such as DocVQA \cite{mathew2021docvqa}, ChartQA \cite{masry2022chartqa}, InfographicVQA \cite{mathew2022infographicvqa}, DUDE \cite{van2023dude}, MP-DocVQA \cite{tito2023hierarchical}, TAT-DQA \cite{zhu2022tatdqa}, MultiHiertt \cite{zhao2022multihiertt}, FUNSD \cite{jaume2019funsd}, OCR-VQA \cite{mishra2019ocrvqa}, and TextVQA \cite{singh2019textvqa} into a unified multi-task mix. Capability-targeted fine-tuning instead curates a single corpus, typically synthesised by a strong LVLM, as in Leopard-Instruct (925K text-rich multi-image instances) \cite{jia2024leopard}, the CoR-Dataset (26K reasoning-trace QA pairs) \cite{guo2026end}, MoLoRAG's logical-relevance corpus \cite{wu2025molorag} (5.5K GPT-4o-synthesised pairs over MMLongBench-Doc \cite{ma2024mmlongbench} and LongDocURL \cite{deng2025longdocurl} PDFs), and DocDancer trajectories \cite{zhang2026docdancer}. Reinforcement and preference learning is emerging at small scale through DocR1's evidence-page-guided GRPO \cite{xiong2026docr1} and CoR's DPO pairs \cite{guo2026end}. A defining feature of this stage is heavy reuse of evaluation datasets for training; MP-DocVQA appears in 15 fine-tuning mixes and 17 evaluation suites, DUDE in 12 and 16, and DocVQA in 9 and 14 respectively (full per-paper attributions in Table~\ref{tab:datasets_used}), with implications discussed in \S\ref{sec:challenges}.

Single-page benchmarks (DocVQA \cite{mathew2021docvqa}, InfographicVQA \cite{mathew2022infographicvqa}, ChartQA \cite{masry2022chartqa}, VisualMRC \cite{tanaka2021visualmrc}, TextVQA \cite{singh2019textvqa}, FUNSD \cite{jaume2019funsd}, and CORD \cite{park2019cord}) originated the field and now mainly serve as sanity checks.
