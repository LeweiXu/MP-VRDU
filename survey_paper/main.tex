\section{Framework Architecture Overview}
%Previous VRDU surveys \cite{ding2025survey} categorise models by OCR dependency, separating OCR-dependent models that consume text and layout boxes from OCR-free models that consume document images directly \cite{huang2022layoutlmv3}. This taxonomy is less informative in the multi-page setting, as OCR-using models often use extracted text as just one component in a multimodal pipeline \cite{sun2025docagent, wang2025vidorag}, while OCR-free models are often pretrained or aligned with OCR-derived supervision \cite{tanaka2025vdocrag, hu2024mplug}. The defining challenge in MP-VRDU is instead how a system manages document-scale evidence while bounded by context window limits. Answer-bearing evidence is typically sparse, distributed across pages, and multimodal in nature, so models must decide what information to retain, what to discard, and how the surviving information should interact across pages. These decisions are governed by architectural design, which determines how evidence is selected, preserved, aggregated, and exposed for reasoning. We therefore organise multi-page frameworks primarily by architectural trend, with OCR dependency as a secondary descriptor.
Visually-rich document understanding has predominantly been studied at the single-page level, where the dominant taxonomy distinguishes OCR-dependent methods, which consume recognised text and layout boxes, from OCR-free methods, which process document images directly~\citep{ding2025survey,huang2022layoutlmv3}. This OCR axis captures the most salient design choice in single-page settings, where the entire document is supplied as one input and methods differ primarily in how textual content is acquired. Multi-page VRDU introduces design dimensions that the OCR axis cannot resolve. As documents extend to tens of pages, two pressures arise simultaneously: the input often exceeds the backbone's context window, and the evidence required to answer a question is sparsely distributed across pages and across modalities. These pressures force systems to make architectural choices about \textit{where} cross-page evidence is selected and integrated, choices that are largely independent of OCR usage and that drive the substantive differences among recent methods. We therefore adopt an evidence-management view of MP-VRDU. Surveying the recent literature through this lens, we identify four architectural trends that recur across systems: page-to-document transformers, backbone-centric MLLM adaptation, retriever-generator systems, and agentic pipelines.

% Earlier VRDU work typically separates OCR-dependent and OCR-free models according to whether they consume OCR tokens and layout boxes or read document images directly \cite{huang2022layoutlmv3, ding2025survey}. In MP-VRDU, this boundary becomes less binary, as OCR is often only one signal within a larger document-level system. It can support sparse retrieval, page grounding, bounding-box references, and textual verification, while visual encoders and VLMs handle image-based evidence \cite{han2025mdocagent, zhu2025simple, wang2025vidorag}. OCR remains valuable as structured text and layout coordinates provide compact representations for text-heavy or low-resolution pages; for example, LayTokenLLM projects OCR tokens, bounding boxes, and layout cues into the language-model space for efficient document reasoning \cite{zhu2025simple}. However, this compactness comes with OCR error propagation and parser dependence \cite{liao2025doclayllm}, and multi-page OCR-based systems still require retrieval, compression, or aggregation mechanisms to control document length \cite{hannan2025docslm, zhang2024cream}.\cite{hannan2025docslm, zhang2024cream}.

\subsection{Page-to-document Encoding Models}
% \textbf{Page-to-Document Encoding Models} encode each page through a shared document encoder including page level OCR text, layout, and visual features, then connect page representations through a document-level exchange mechanism such as summary tokens \cite{}, document-global tokens \cite{}, recurrent memory \cite{}, or sparse cross-page attention \cite{}. Cross-page interaction occur within the framework in a single forward pass, while page identity, local layout, and reading order remain explicit, providing a strong inductive bias for multi-page structure. Explicit cross-page modelling makes these models well suited to tasks where relevant evidence appears across pages within a coherent document structure, such as reports and business documents \cite{tito2023hierarchical, borchmann2025arctic}. Its limitations follow from the same design, as document-level representations may lose-fine grained evidence when page information is compressed or weakly connected \cite{blau2024gram, dong2024multi}. Moreover, computation grows with document length as all pages typically need to be encoded, making very long documents difficult to scale.
Page-to-document encoding models process each page through a shared layout-aware encoder (e.g., LayoutLMv3 or DocFormerv2) that fuses OCR text, bounding-box layout, and visual features within the page, then connect per-page representations through cross-page attention~\citep{blau2024gram,borchmann2025arctic}, per-page summary tokens~\citep{tito2023hierarchical}, or recurrent state propagation~\citep{dong2024multi}. By preserving page boundaries and cross-page reading order through architecture rather than learning them from data, this design suits moderately long documents with regular structural templates such as multi-page forms and reports, but couples cost to document length: every page must be jointly encoded, and the page-to-document compression step discards fine-grained evidence when scaled to longer inputs~\citep{blau2024gram,dong2024multi}.

% Rather than flattening all tokens and visual patches into one sequence, these models preserve page boundaries while connecting pages through summary tokens \cite{tito2023hierarchical}, global document tokens with local-global attention \cite{blau2024gram}, chunked sparse attention over long OCR streams \cite{borchmann2025arctic}, or recurrent memory passed across pages \cite{dong2024multi}. This design provides an inductive bias for multi-page understanding as evidence can be aggregated across pages without discarding page identity. However, cross-page reasoning is still bounded by the exchange mechanism, as summaries and memories can lose fine-grained evidence, sparse attention may miss distant dependencies, and computation generally grows with document length.

% This family uses a learned document encoder to separately encode individual pages, then introduces a document-level mechanism for information exchange across page representations. Hi-VT5 encodes each page independently and passes question-conditioned page tokens to the decoder, but its cross-page reasoning is delayed until after page-level compression \cite{tito2023hierarchical}. GRAM addresses this bottleneck by injecting document-global tokens into the encoder so pages can exchange information during representation learning, although its cost still increases with document length and optional compression can reduce answer quality \cite{blau2024gram}. RM-T5 instead carries recurrent memory tokens from page to page, making document aggregation more parameter-efficient, but fixed sequential memory can over-compress or forget earlier evidence \cite{dong2024multi}. Arctic-TILT pushes the same direction to much longer OCR-based inputs through layer-wise multimodal fusion and chunked sparse attention, but remains sensitive to OCR quality, visual layout complexity, and position bias in very long documents \cite{borchmann2025arctic}. These architectures preserve page structure and make document-level interaction explicit, but reasoning over many pages must still pass through compressed page summaries, memory states, or sparse attention patterns.

\subsection{Backbone-Centric MLLM Adaptation}
% For this part, we can emphasie the key point is continue pretraining the MLLM backbone. Then, you can introduce the frameworks from training paradigms updates (full training to parameter efficient tuning) and introducing additional machemisms. 
Backbone-centric MLLM adaptation treats a pretrained multimodal LLM as the entire document understanding model and adapts it to multi-page input. Adaptation strategies vary in how much of the model is updated: some methods train only projectors that map document features into the backbone's token space~\citep{tanaka2024instructdoc} or token-compression modules that contain visual sequence growth~\citep{hu2025mplugdocowl2}, while others fine-tune the backbone itself through continued pretraining~\citep{duan2025docopilot}, instruction tuning, or reinforcement learning~\citep{xiong2026docr1}. Because evidence integration and answer generation happen inside the backbone, this family avoids the retrieval and orchestration infrastructure required by later families and benefits directly from the MLLM's instruction-following and reasoning capabilities. However, this internalisation also defines its limits: the backbone's context length caps total document size, fine-grained text and visual signals depend on how faithfully they survive token compression~\citep{jia2024leopard}, and document structure such as section hierarchy and figure-caption linkage must be recovered implicitly from pixels, since unlike page-to-document encoders this family does not feed structure through dedicated layout tokens or structural encoders~\citep{hannan2025docslm,duan2025docopilot}.
% This family adapts a pretrained MLLM backbone to multi-page input by training projectors that map per-modality features into the backbone's token space \cite{tanaka2024instructdoc}, adaptors that enable parameter-efficient fine-tuning for document understanding \cite{zhu2025simple}, and compression modules that contain visual token growth on long inputs \cite{hu2025mplug}. Some works further unfreeze the backbone for continued pretraining or instruction tuning on document corpora, trading parameter efficiency for stronger document-specific grounding \cite{duan2025docopilot}. These models inherit the instruction-following and multimodal reasoning of the foundation model and remain parameter-efficient to train, fitting tasks that combine document reading with open-ended reasoning and moderate-length documents where long-context tuning massively improves MLLM performance on multi-page documents \cite{hu2024mplug, yu2024texthawk2}. However, as the backbone must absorb document-scale evidence internally, performance degrades beyond moderate-length documents, leaving quality bound to context length and to how faithfully fine-grained text and visual signal survive token compression \cite{jia2024leopard}. Additionally, reasoning over visual elements depends entirely on the backbone's pretrained visual capabilities, and as most models in this family are OCR-free, reliable text recovery requires high-resolution input that aggressive downsampling readily compromises. Moreover, document structure such as section hierarchy and figure-caption linkage must likewise be recovered implicitly from pixels rather than supplied through layout tokens or a structural encoder \cite{hannan2025docslm, duan2025docopilot}.

% Backbone-adapted MP-VRDU methods extend pretrained MLLMs to support multi-page document understanding by modifying the model architecture, training strategy, or input representation. These methods aim to encode page images, OCR text, and layout information into a unified document-level representation. Early approaches mainly conducting continuing pretraining or instruction tuning on projectors or lightweight adapters to align document-specific visual and textual signals with pretrained MLLMs \cite{jia2024leopard, tanaka2024instructdoc}. Subsequent work places greater emphasis on efficient document representation, introducing visual token compression \cite{}, layout-aware encoding, streaming inference, or long-context training to reduce the computational cost of multi-page inputs \cite{hu2025mplug, hannan2025docslm, yu2024texthawk2}. More recent studies further enhance document-level reasoning through large-scale multi-page supervision or evidence-guided reinforcement learning \cite{duan2025docopilot, xiong2026docr1}. Overall, this paradigm preserves the general instruction-following and multimodal reasoning capabilities of foundation models while adapting them to the structural and evidential demands of long documents. Its main limitation is that critical evidence must still be encoded and retained within the backbone, making performance highly dependent on context length, compression quality, OCR–vision alignment, and the preservation of fine-grained cross-page information.

\subsection{Retriever-Generator}
% For this part you can describe based on retrieving content (granuliary), retrieving methods (dense, sparse, joint, grep) and then emphasing the correlation between chunks. 
Retriever-generator systems divide MP-VRDU into evidence selection and answer generation, where a retrieval module returns a compact subset of OCR chunks, pages, visual patches, multimodal elements, or graph nodes, and a generator answers conditioned only on those units \cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag}. Inference cost depends on the retrieved content rather than document length, allowing this architecture to scale to documents that exceed any current MLLM context window. Modular decomposition allows the retriever and generator to be swapped independently while retrieved evidence provides a partial audit trail for the answer. Furthermore, the same interface extends from a single document to a corpus, supporting multi-document question answering without significant architectural change. Structured domains with reliable retrieval anchors such as scientific papers and technical manuals benefit most \cite{shin2025multidocfusion}, and visually rich pages benefit further when page-level visual retrieval preserves figures, tables, and layout that OCR-only pipelines lose \cite{tanaka2025vdocrag, xie2024wukong}. However, evidence missed at retrieval is unrecoverable at generation, and broadening the retrieved set to compensate introduces noise and attention dilution. Additionally, performance depends on parser and embedding quality, while chunking errors, OCR mistakes, and retriever-generator modality mismatches propagate directly into the answer. The single-pass design also presupposes that relevant evidence is rankable by query similarity, which weakens when answers require aggregating pages that are not individually strongly relevant \cite{wang2024knowledge, wu2025molorag}.

% This family decomposes MP-VRDU into evidence selection and answer generation in which a retrieval module first identifies a compact subset of relevant document content, and a generator answers using only the selected evidence. Retrieved units may be OCR chunks, pages, visual patches, multmimodal elements, or graph nodes, depending on whether the document is represented as text, images, interleaved content, or a structured evidence graph. Retrieval can be performed through course-to-fine selection \cite{zhang2024cream}, adaptive visual retrieval \cite{li2025avir}, multimodal dense retrieval \cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag}, end-to-end sparse sampling over paragraphs and figures \cite{xie2024wukong}, graph-based prompting \cite{wang2024knowledge}, or logic-aware retrieval \cite{wu2025molorag}. This architecture scales well by preventing the generator from processing the full document, while the selected evidence provides partial interpretability. However, this introduces a retrieval bottleneck in which missed evidence is usually unrecoverable at generation time, while adding more retrieved context can introduce noise or attention dilution.

%     \item These systems can retrieve over OCR text, page images, multimodal chunks, or structured indexes such as graphs, and some models blur the boundary with native MLLMs by training the retriever or sampler end-to-end with the generator.

\subsection{Agentic Pipeline}
Recent surveys \cite{plaat2025agentic, wang2024survey} characterise agentic LLM systems through their capacity to reason, act, and interact with an external environment beyond a single forward pass. Following this framing, we categorise MP-VRDU systems that perform multi-pass evidence acquisition or distributes work across specialised agents as agentic, while reserving the retriever-generator family for single-pass models. Agentic systems thus treat MP-VRDU as an iterative evidence-seeking process, where one or more controller MLLMs decide what to inspect next from intermediate observations and issue tool calls for retrieval, navigation, cropping, or OCR. Roles such as retrieval, inspection, synthesis, and adjudication may be distributed across agents, with each agent backed by a different backbone model selected for its subtask \cite{han2025mdocagent} rather than tied to a single generator in retriever-generator systems. This design lifts the single-pass bottleneck, allows compute to scale with question difficulty rather than document length, and the trajectory itself becomes an inspectable audit trail. This suits questions whose evidence is distributed across pages or modalities and multi-hop questions that need query reformulation between steps \cite{wang2025vidorag, sun2025docagent}, and visually heterogeneous documents whose text, tables, and figures benefit from tool-specific inspection \cite{han2025mdocagent, zhu2025doclens}. However, adaptability in compute is difficult to bound before inference, and action policies and selection criteria remain sensitive to prompt phrasing, with silent failure when the controller commits early to wrong evidence. Moreover, reproducibility is complicated by stochastic generation and the absence of a single attributable forward pass, and the system inherits the recall ceiling of every retriever, parser, or OCR module it calls.

% This family treats MP-VRDU as an iterative evidence seeking process rather than a fixed retrieve-then-generate pass. In this survey, this category refers to systems that use multi-agent decomposition, tool use, memory, or open-ended control to navigate long documents, inspect evidence, refine queries, and revise answers. Some pipelines assign retrieval, visual inspection, modal fusion, judgement and answer synthesis across specialised agents \cite{han2025mdocagent, duan2025m2rag, yu2025visual}, or use tool-augment and dynamic iterative workflows to navigate documents, inspect evidence, maintain intermediate states, and revise answers \cite{sun2025docagent, zhu2025doclens, zheng2026doc, wang2025vidorag}. This design is effective when evidence is scattered, visually fine-grained, or missed by a single retrieval step, allowing the model to gather additional context before answer generation. However, this introduces higher latency, repeated model calls, dependence on external parsing or tool quality, and greater difficulty in reproducing and evaluating the full decision process.

\section{Multi-Page Modalities Overview}
% MP-VRDU models must model text, visual elements, and layout over multiple pages. Hierarchical models usually encode modalities locally within each page before document-level aggregation. Backbone-centric MLLMs project or compress multimodal page evidence into the backbone context. Retrieval-generator and agentic pipeline models convert modalities into searchable or navigable evidence units.

\subsection{Text Modality}
Text is the most information-dense modality in MP-VRDU, carrying the bulk of a document's explicit semantic content in a compact, searchable form, obtained either through OCR or recovered directly from page pixels by an LVLM. Extracted text from OCR supports cheap indexing and retrieval over documents that exceed context windows, natural answer-passage attribution, and bridges to visual elements via captions and references. However, this comes at the cost of OCR error propagation, fragile multi-column and table reading order, and the loss of chart, layout, and handwriting semantics. Page-to-document transformers typically rely on this acquisition method, treating OCR-extracted text as the primary per-page input, encoded jointly with layout and visual embeddings and exchanged across pages through document-level aggregation \cite{tito2023hierarchical, blau2024gram, borchmann2025arctic}. In contrast, pixel-recovered text avoids OCR dependence but demands high-resolution input and extensive text-recognition pretraining, both of which scale poorly with document length. Backbone-centric MLLMs typically rely on this method, reading text through the backbone's internal vision capabilities, applied to whole-document context in long-context variants \cite{hu2025mplug, yu2024texthawk2} and selectively to retrieved regions in others \cite{zheng2026doc, jain2025simpledoc}. Retriever-generators use text as a searchable index over chunks, pages, or summaries and as evidence passed to the generator alongside or in place of visual inputs \cite{zhang2024cream, shin2025multidocfusion}, while agentic pipelines additionally use it as a navigation layer where outlines and section searches direct later visual inspection \cite{han2025mdocagent, sun2025docagent, wang2025vidorag}.

% \paragraph{OCR Text as Page-Level Representation.} 
% Early hierarchical document transformer models generally treat OCR text as a structured page representation rather than as plain text. Work tokens are paired with bounding boxes, page identifiers, visual patches, and query-conditioned summary or memory tokens. \subsection{Visual Modality}
Visual elements in MP-VRDU range from text-bearing structures such as dense paargraphs and tables to non-textual evidence such as charts, figures, and handwriting that OCR cannot recover. Visual token cost scales with both page count and per-page resolution, making full document high-resolution encoding infeasible at realistic document length. This forces every architecture to trade page coverage against reading fidelity within a fixed token budget. Some models \cite{hu2025mplug, zheng2026doc, jain2025simpledoc} keep all pages available at low resolution to preserve document-scale visual evidence at low token cost while relying on OCR or parsed text to supply finer reading where the down-sampled image is insufficient. Others \cite{hu2025mplug, guo2026end} sidestep this tradeoff through selective high-resolution reading, restricting expensive visual encoding to pages or regions surfaced by retrieval, navigation, or controller driven inspection. Retriever-generator systems \cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag, li2025avir} take a different route, often use visual page embeddings purely as a retrieval signal rather than as a reading input. These approaches are not mutually exclusive, and many systems \cite{zheng2026doc, jain2025simpledoc} pair low-resolution thumbnails for navigation with selective high-resolution inspection of pages a retriever or tool-call surfaces. Each strategy, however, carries a characteristic failure mode. Low-resolution context loses fine text and small visual elements, selective high-resolution reading inherits the recall ceiling of whichever tool chooses what to read, and visual retrieval can rank pages whose layout looks relevant without confirming the answer-bearing region is present. Compression and tokenisation choices further determine how reliably embedded text, table cells, and figure annotations survive into the model input.

\subsection{Layout Modality}
%markdown and html (strcuture formating is important) not layout modality only more like structure information. 
Layout in MP-VRDU spans two levels. Within a page, it captures reading order, columns, table structure, and bounding-box geometry, largely shared with single-page VRDU and covered in prior surveys \cite{ding2025survey}. Across pages, it captures document-level structure such as section hierarchy, page order, multi-page tables, and cross-references, which is the distinctive multi-page problem. Within-page layout is largely handled by mechanisms inherited from single-page VRDU, either through layout-aware pretraining objectives \cite{tito2023hierarchical, hu2025mplug, zhu2025simple} or through implicit recovery from page pixels by an OCR-free MLLM \cite{yu2024texthawk2}. Cross-page layout is rarely captured by these mechanisms, as per-token spatial annotations describe individual tokens rather than relationships between sections, pages, or references, and pretraining supervision for document-scale structure is expensive to obtain. A growing line of work therefore exposes parsed structural information directly to the model. Section trees and heading hierarchies are extracted by a structural parser and used to chunk documents along semantic boundaries before retrieval, so that retrieved units respect section scope rather than sliding-window cuts \cite{shin2025multidocfusion}. Hierarchical retrieval indices route queries first to coarse levels such as sections or chapters and then to fine-grained units within the selected branch, reducing irrelevant pages and preserving cross-page context \cite{gong2025mhier, zhang2026mldocrag}. Knowledge graphs constructed over headings, entities, and cross-references convert layout into a navigable structure, allowing controllers to traverse from a starting page to related pages through typed edges rather than dense similarity alone \cite{wang2024knowledge}. Each route carries a distinct cost. Layout-as-objective demands pretraining supervision that is expensive at multi-page scale, implicit visual layout depends on backbone capability and high-resolution input, and explicit structure depends on parser quality, with errors in heading detection, table boundaries, or reading-order extraction propagating into every downstream retrieval and navigation step.

\subsection{Modality Fusion}
Prior MLLM VRDU surveys cover fusion mechanisms such as neural, target-oriented, and prompt-based fusion at length \cite{ding2025survey}. In MP-VRDU, the more distinctive variation is the timing of the fusion, as full text-vision-layout fusion across every page is computationally infeasible at document scale, so systems differ on whether modalities fuse inside the encoder or only after evidence has been selected. Some models \cite{tito2023hierarchical, blau2024gram, borchmann2025arctic} use early fusion, combining text, visual, and layout features within each page during encoding. Page-to-document transformers fuse modalities locally before document-level aggregation, and backbone-centric MLLMs fuse modalities via adaptors or projectors that map multimodal features into the LLM token space \cite{tanaka2024instructdoc, jia2024leopard}. Early fusion preserves fine-grained multimodal alignment but is costly when many pages must be processed jointly. Other models \cite{} use late fusion, keeping modalities separate during retrieval or inspection and combining them only after candidate evidence has been selected. Retriever-generator pipelines may identify relevant text passages first and bring in visual evidence afterwards \cite{zhang2024cream, gong2025mhier}, and agentic systems extend the pattern by utilizing separate agents to inspect text, tables, and figures before a synthesis or adjudication step \cite{han2025mdocagent, duan2025m2rag, zhu2025doclens}. As such, late fusion scales better to long documents but depends strongly on retrieval and routing quality. Other multi-page systems sit between these poles, fusing modalities locally within pages and again across selected evidence at the document level.

\section{Training Strategies}
Training strategies in MP-VRDU are closely tied to how each architecture controls the cost and complexity of multi-page document understanding. While the previous sections describe how models represent documents and combine text, visual, and layout evidence, this section focuses on how these capabilities are learned.

\paragraph{Parameter efficienct tuning on frozen backbones}
A common strategy is to reuse strong pretrained LLMs or LVLMs and update only a small number of task-specific components. Rather than training vision encoders or language backbones end-to-end, these methods freeze most pretrained parameters and introduce lightweight modules such as LoRA, projectors, adapters, layout tokenizers, or task-specific heads. 
LayTokenLLM~\cite{Zhu2025-jb-laytokenllm} exemplifies this by keeping the LLM backbone frozen and introducing a lightweight layout tokenization module together with LoRA-based adaptation. Rather than modifying the architecture, it injects layout information into the input sequence using a compressed representation and a specialized positional encoding scheme that avoids additional context overhead, enabling efficient scaling to long multi-page documents. As an alternative, some work adopts retrieval-augmented strategies, which avoid directly modeling entire documents by selecting only the most relevant content for downstream reasoning. CREAM~\cite{Zhang2024-zs-cream} introduces a coarse-to-fine retrieval framework. It first retrieves candidate text chunks based on embedding similarity, and then applies multi-round LLM-based re-ranking to identify the most relevant evidence. Extending this idea, MoLoRAG~\cite{Wu2025-mi-molorag} incorporates logical reasoning into retrieval by constructing a graph over document pages and performing multi-hop traversal, enabling the model to capture both semantic and logical relevance across pages.

\paragraph{Curriculum / Multi-Stage Training}
Directly adapting single-page models to multi-page objectives is often ineffective and does not scale well, as it fails to capture cross-page dependencies and long-context interactions.%Either directly adapt single page model, then train on multi-page objectives but doesn't scale well.
Instead, many approaches adopt a progressive training strategy, where models are first trained on simpler local tasks and then gradually extended to more complex multi-page objectives, improving both performance and training efficiency.

A representative example is DocSLM~\cite{Hannan2025-zu}, which adopts multi-stage curriculum. The training begins with image-OCR alignment, followed by stages that introduce image-level and document-level compression, and finally extends to instruction tuning and reasoning. mPLUG-DocOwl2~\cite{Hu2024-wl} follows a related staged training strategy, but with an OCR-free multi-image focus. It first performs single-image structure learning to ensure that compressed visual tokens preserve document text and layout information, then pretrain on multi-page parsing.

Beyond architectural design, another key challenge lies in effective retrieval mechanisms. Rather than encoding entire documents, many approaches adopt retrieval-augmented pipelines, where models first select relevant pages or segments before performing reasoning. In this setting, the performance of the system is heavily dependent on the quality of the retriever. For example, VDocRAG~\cite{Tanaka2025-er} introduces self-supervised representation compression retrieval that aligns visual document representations with OCR-derived textual content, enabling large vision-language models to learn document embeddings directly from unlabeled document-image pairs.

Agentic methods iteratively explore document pages, refine intermediate reasoning steps, and dynamically select evidence. \section{Training-Free Strategies}
Training-free strategies improve MP-VRDU performance without updating the parameters of the underlying LLM and VLM backbones. Strategies include offline document-side preparation, such as parsing the document into a graph, tree, or outline, as well as online inference-side orchestrations through prompting, tool use, retrieval refinement, and multi-agent coordination. Compared to supervised training, retrieval tuning, and reinforcement learning, training-free strategies remain less systematically explored despite their growing prevalence in agentic and retrieval-based systems for multi-page document understanding.

\subsection{Retrieval and Navigation Techniques}
Training-free retrieval uses off-the-shelf encoders, document structure, or LLMs as routers and re-rankers to select page or chunk evidence, contrasting with supervised retrievers covered previously. Retrieval quality bounds every downstream training-free reasoning method, so the choice of retrieval signal dominates system behaviour on long documents. 

\paragraph{Sparse Hierarchy-Aware Search.}

Many models parse the input document into an explicit textual structure and perform sparse retrieval or traversal through term or structure anchored matching. MultiDocFusion~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion} uses a hierarchy parser to recover document, section, and subsection trees from scanned PDFs and groups passages along DFS-ordered Markdown headers, so retrieved chunks preserve their parent context (parser tuned). DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} instead extracts a tree-formatted XML outline from headings, sections, and captions and exposes it through section-level search and section-content fetch tools that navigate the parsed hierarchy directly. MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} extends the structure to a dual hierarchy of in-page flattened chunks and cross-page topological chunks, with LLM-based re-ranking across both indices, addressing the cross-page fragmentation that single-tree designs leave unresolved. However, sparse signals match on lexical or structural overlap and miss semantic connections and paraphrased queries, resulting in degraded performance on questions whose phrasing diverges from the document's surface form.

% Many models parse the input document into an explicit textual structure and perform sparse retrieval or traversal through term or structure anchored matching. MultiDocFusion~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion} uses a hierarchy parser to recover document, section, and subsection trees from scanned PDFs and groups passages along DFS-ordered Markdown headers (parser tuned), preserving parent context but fixing the groupings at parse time so retrieval cannot adapt to the query. DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} addresses this by extracting a tree-formatted XML outline from headings, sections, and captions and exposing it through section-level search and fetch tools that an agent navigates query-by-query, although the single hierarchy still treats sections as disjoint and cannot follow references between pages. MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} closes this gap with a dual hierarchy of in-page flattened chunks and cross-page topological chunks,and LLM-based re-ranking across both indices, recovering links that single-tree designs leave unresolved. However, sparse signals match only on lexical or structural overlap and miss semantic connections and paraphrased queries, resulting in degraded performance on questions whose phrasing diverges from the document's surface form.

\paragraph{Dense Embedding Search.}
Dense embedding retrieval addresses this by encoding pages or chunks into learned representations, so queries match on semantic similarity rather than lexical or structural overlap. Many models \cite{wu2025doc, wu2025molorag, han2025mdocagent, jain2025simpledoc, wang2025vidorag} adopt vision-language late-interaction encoders such as ColPali, ColBERT, and ColQwen as their dense retriever. These encoders embed the query as a sequence of contextual token vectors and each page as a bag of patch embeddings produced by a vision-language transformer over the rendered page image, then score a query-page pair by taking the maximum cosine similarity of each query token over all page patches and summing the result. MaxSim aggregation preserves fine-grained alignment between specific query terms and specific page regions, which is critical in MP-VRDU as a question token may align with a single figure caption, table cell, or paragraph buried in a long document. M3DocRAG~\cite{cho2024m3docrag} instantiates this pattern by retrieving at chunk and page granularity with the rest of the pipeline frozen. AVIR~\cite{li2025avir} adds an adaptive page selector that examines the per-query score distribution and either thresholds at a fixed cutoff for short documents or applies K-means clustering with a top-K bound on longer ones, so the number of pages handed to the reasoner scales with the score distribution rather than being fixed (lightweight head trained). Such methods handle paraphrase and cross-modal queries well, yet remain structure-blind on their own, since a section that should be retrieved as a unit can be split across embedding chunks and cross-page references are not represented as first-class objects.

\paragraph{Joint Multi-Signal Search.}
The complementary strengths of sparse and dense signals can be combined at the retrieval stage rather than committing to a single channel. CREAM pairs BGE coarse retrieval with iterative LLM-based re-ranking across overlapping chunk groups, so dense semantic candidates are filtered by an LLM judgement that draws on the full passage text \cite{zhang2024cream}. SimpleDoc combines ColPali page embeddings with LLM-generated summary re-ranking under a working memory that persists across rounds, so successive iterations can refine the joint score \cite{jain2025simpledoc}. MDocAgent runs parallel text and image retrieval tracks whose candidates are aggregated by a downstream agent, joining textual and visual evidence rather than fusing modalities at encoding time \cite{han2025mdocagent}. Such combinations improve recall by spanning what any single signal misses, but still score each candidate passage in isolation, so retrieval cannot follow logical links, cross-references, or multi-hop dependencies that span passages.

% The complementary strengths of sparse and dense signals can be combined at the retrieval stage rather than committing to a single channel. CREAM~\cite{zhang2024cream} pairs BGE coarse text retrieval with iterative LLM-based re-ranking across overlapping chunk groups, filtering dense semantic candidates through an LLM judgement that draws on the full passage text, although both signals operate over text alone and cannot surface evidence buried in figures, tables, or rendered layout. SimpleDoc~\cite{jain2025simpledoc} addresses this by combining ColPali page embeddings with LLM-generated summary re-ranking under a working memory that persists across rounds, so visual layout and a text-form page abstraction jointly contribute to the score, although the two cues collapse into a single per-page judgement and the modalities are never weighed against each other independently. MDocAgent~\cite{han2025mdocagent} goes further by running parallel text and image retrieval tracks each with its own retriever and aggregating their candidates at the agent level rather than fusing modalities at encoding, so the agent sees one ranked list per modality and can route the question to the modality whose evidence is strongest, although both rankings still treat each candidate passage in isolation. Such combinations improve performance across signals and modalities, yet retrieval still cannot follow logical links, cross-references, or multi-hop dependencies that span passages.

\paragraph{Graph-Based Traversal}
Graph traversal addresses this by indexing the document as a graph and navigating it through edges, resulting in strong inter-passage. KGP~\cite{wang2024knowledge} constructs a knowledge graph over passages and document-structure nodes such as pages, paragraphs, and tables, with edges encoding lexical similarity and structural belonging, then uses an instruction-tuned LLM as a traverser that predicts the next evidence node from the visited path. MoLoRAG~\cite{wu2025molorag} instead builds a page graph in which edge candidates are filtered by embedding similarity, then scores logical relevance with a frozen Qwen2.5-VL prompt and combines that with semantic similarity for multi-hop traversal, so retrieval can follow logical dependencies that surface similarity misses. MLDocRAG~\cite{zhang2026mldocrag} constructs the graph per query, anchoring chunk nodes to LVLM-generated query nodes and expanding through KNN edges between queries, so the structure adapts to the question rather than being fixed at index time. Graph methods reach references that scoring methods cannot, but results in construction overhead, edge-quality dependence, and traversal cost that scales with hop count.

\subsection{Inference-Time Reasoning Strategies}
Inference-time reasoning strategies modify the reasoning behaviour of a frozen LLM or VLM through prompting, sampling, and action-selection mechanisms applied at inference rather than through parameter updates.

\paragraph{Chain-of-Thought and Self-Improvement.}
Chain-of-thought prompting elicits intermediate reasoning steps from a frozen LLM or VLM that integrate retrieved text, tables, and figures and combine facts across pages before producing the final answer. Following this definition by \cite{wei2022chain}, the entire reasoning chain runs over a fixed evidence buffer assembled by a prior retrieval step or over the entire compressed document, so the reasoner cannot acquire new or finer grained evidence once decoding begins. MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} applies a structured CoT prompt to its hierarchical retrieval output, although a single reasoning chain remains vulnerable to compounding errors on dense layouts. SimpleDoc~\cite{jain2025simpledoc} interleaves self-refinement and self-verification, emitting a refined query when evidence is judged insufficient, although the loop terminates on the backbone's own judgement and inherits its blind spots. DocSLM~\cite{hannan2025docslm} (trained, see \S4) instead applies document-level self-consistency, streaming page segments and selecting the lowest-uncertainty answer from a learned uncertainty head, trading compute for robustness against single-pass errors. DocR1~\cite{xiong2026docr1} (trained, see \S4) internalises the CoT trajectory through reinforcement learning, producing structured \texttt{<think>}, \texttt{<evidence\_page>}, and \texttt{<answer>} outputs that interleave reasoning with explicit evidence localisation. Across these variants, reasoning operates over a fixed evidence buffer, so questions whose evidence sits outside the retrieved set remain unreachable regardless of how the trace is sampled or verified.

\paragraph{ReAct and Tool-Augmented Reasoning.}
ReAct~\cite{yao2022react} interleaves reasoning and acting, alternating intermediate reasoning steps with tool calls whose observations feed the next step. In MP-VRDU the tools retrieve, fetch, or inspect document pages, letting the reasoner gather evidence during inference rather than work from a closed buffer. Doc-React~\cite{wu2025doc} instantiates flat-retrieval ReAct with InfoNCE-guided sub-query refinement and entropy-based termination, although each iteration still selects from a single retriever's ranked list and recovery from a poor encoder match relies on subsequent query rewrites. Outline-driven variants combine this loop with the structured navigation of \S5.1. DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} traverses its tree-formatted XML outline through section-level search and fetch tools chosen turn-by-turn by an actor agent, and DocDancer~\cite{zhang2026docdancer} (trajectory-trained) extends this with explicit intent modelling so action choices are conditioned on the agent's evolving goal, although both inherit the parser's outline quality and degrade on documents without exploitable structure. Doc-V$^*$~\cite{zheng2026doc} (RL-trained) couples ReAct with a thumbnail-level document map, alternating semantic search with direct page indexing under a working memory of summaries. ReAct loops sharpen evidence localisation through dynamic action selection, yet pay in tool latency that grows with rounds, prompt sensitivity at each step, and a fixed-resolution reading of any fetched page that leaves fine-grained content exposed to attention dilution.

% \paragraph{Coarse-to-Fine Visual Reading.}
% Coarse-to-fine visual reading addresses the fixed-resolution limit by inspecting retrieved pages selectively at multiple scales rather than as whole images. Doc-V$^*$~\cite{zheng2026doc} (RL-trained, see \S4) embodies the agentic form, maintaining a thumbnail-level overview and applying high-resolution reading only on regions returned by retrieval or fetch actions. DocLens~\cite{zhu2025doclens} and ViDoRAG~\cite{wang2025vidorag} apply the same split through multi-agent pipelines, with DocLens separating a page navigator from an element localiser that crops layout regions before reasoning, and ViDoRAG pairing a coarse seeker with a fine-resolution inspector that emits reflective feedback. CoR~\cite{guo2026end} (trained, see \S4) realises the same pattern within a single MLLM forward pass through phased and focused search over rendered pages. Such designs concentrate compute on task-relevant regions, yet make reasoning conditional on the localiser, since the fine pass cannot recover when the coarse pass misroutes. Across the three families, prompt sensitivity, tool latency, and the compounding effect of an early wrong step on later reasoning remain shared bottlenecks regardless of loop shape.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Decomposition}
Multi-agent decomposition assigns specialised LLM and VLM roles such as modality readers, retrievers, judges, and synthesisers to a single question and coordinates them through a fixed pipeline, controller, or critic loop.

\paragraph{Modality-Specialised Pipelines.}
Some models specialise agents by input modality or by stage of the evidence pipeline and aggregates their outputs through a final synthesis. MDocAgent~\cite{han2025mdocagent} runs a five-agent pipeline in which a general agent provides initial multimodal context, a critical agent extracts key evidence from both modalities, dedicated text and image agents read their respective tracks in parallel, and a summariser fuses the four outputs into a single answer. M2RAG~\cite{duan2025m2rag} splits work between a text filter, a visual extractor, and a modal fuser that applies a confidence-conditional rule, falling back to one modality when the other reports no information and fusing only when both contribute (LoRA trained). Parallel pipelines improve modality coverage compared to single-track retrieval, but lack an error-correction stage, so an incorrect output from one branch can dominate aggregation when the other branch lacks counter-evidence.

\paragraph{Verification and Adjudication.}
Other models addresses this challenge by inserting an explicit verifier between proposal and answer. DocLens~\cite{zhu2025doclens} samples multiple candidate answers from a reasoning module and routes them through an adjudicator that compares reasoning paths and selects the most consistent conclusion, so candidates are judged against each other rather than fused unconditionally. DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} pairs an actor with a reviewer that cross-checks through complementary tools, with low tool-overlap reported between the two agents, and writes any disagreement into a shared reflection memory that updates the actor's behaviour on subsequent rounds. Adjudication catches single-branch errors that fusion cannot, but the verifier draws on the same backbone family as the actor, so shared blind spots persist and the verifier can over-reject correct candidates under noisy tool output.

\paragraph{Adaptive Orchestration.}
A third family treats the loop itself as adaptive, allocating compute per question and per role rather than running a fixed pipeline. ViDoRAG~\cite{wang2025vidorag} cycles a seeker that retrieves coarse candidates, an inspector that reviews them at fine resolution and emits reflective feedback, and an answerer that performs a consistency check, with a Gaussian mixture model setting per-query K bounds for each modality from the similarity distribution rather than using a static top-K. MACT~\cite{yu2025visual} decomposes the loop further into planning, execution, judgement, and answer agents, separating the judge from the corrector to avoid the same agent excusing its own errors, and applies agent-wise adaptive test-time scaling so compute is allocated per role and per question (RL-trained). Adaptive orchestration improves the accuracy-latency trade-off but results in latency that scales jointly with the number of rounds and agents, a limitation shared across all multi-agent designs.

% Training free strategies improve MP-VRDU by adapting inference rather than model parameters, but remain less systematically explored than supervised training, retrieval tuning, or reinforcement learning. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting uses a frozen LLM or VLM to decompose a document question into intermediate reasoning steps \cite{wei2022chain}, while ReAct can be viewed as tool-augment CoT, where reasoning steps are interleaved with actions such as search, page retrieval, crop inspection, or query reformulation \cite{yao2022react, wu2025doc}. Structure-aware retrieval uses document organisation, including headings, outlines, page links, summaries, or graph nodes, to guide evidence selection beyond flat top-k ranking \cite{shin2025multidocfusion, wu2025molorag}. Multi-agent decomposition assigns complementary roles such as retrieval, visual inspection, answer synthesis, review, or adjudication to separately prompted LLM agents \cite{han2025mdocagent, zhu2025doclens, wang2025vidorag}. These methods can improve evidence localisation and cross-page aggregation without additional training, but depend on prompt design, retrieval accuracy, tool quality and latency, and coordination reliability for agentic decomposition.

\section{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}
MP-VRDU data has moved from a single-benchmark training-and-evaluation loop toward a layered pipeline. Pretraining draws on large heterogeneous document corpora, fine-tuning is increasingly model-curated through synthetic generation, and benchmarks specialise in stressing document length, multimodal evidence, and evidence localisation.

\subsection{Pretraining Datasets}
The goal of MP-VRDU pretraining is to learn OCR-free reading, layout sensitivity, and high-resolution page representations, with multi-page capability typically emerging from interleaved or concatenated long documents rather than from explicit multi-page supervision. General-purpose archives remain the dominant substrate. IIT-CDIP \cite{lewis2006iitcdip} provides over 42 million scanned pages from the Legacy Tobacco Documents Library and is widely used for self-supervised text--layout--visual pretraining \cite{tito2023hierarchical, borchmann2025arctic}. CCpdf \cite{turski2023ccpdf} extracts millions of multilingual PDFs from Common Crawl with paired OCR and layout annotations, and OCR-IDL \cite{biten2022ocridl} releases roughly 26 million OCR annotations over the Industry Documents Library. Layout-aware image--text corpora target visual reading more directly. DocStruct4M \cite{hu2024mplug} provides 4 million structure-rich document images reused by mPLUG-DocOwl2 for high-resolution compression pretraining \cite{hu2025mplug}. Multi-page-specific corpora are more recent. MP-DocStruct1M \cite{hu2025mplug} contributes around 1 million multi-page parsing and lookup samples, Doc-750K \cite{duan2025docopilot} curates 758K long Sci-Hub, arXiv, and OpenReview documents to train Docopilot natively at the multi-page level, and DocGenome \cite{xia2024docgenome} aggregates around 500K scientific articles with hierarchical structure annotations. Despite these additions, multi-page-specific pretraining substrate remains thin compared with single-page document collections.

\subsection{Fine-Tuning and Instruction Datasets}
Fine-tuning data specialises pretrained backbones to multi-page reading, retrieval, and reasoning, and is where the shift toward model-curated supervision is most visible since per-page annotation of long documents does not scale to current architectures. Instruction-tuning collections aggregate prior DocVQA-family supervision into broader skill sets. InstructDoc \cite{tanaka2024instructdoc} unifies 30 document datasets across 12 tasks under a shared instruction format, and Leopard-Instruct \cite{jia2024leopard} provides 1.2 million text-rich multi-image instructions covering slides, scientific figures, and webpages. Multi-page-specific instruction corpora include MP-DocReason51K \cite{hu2025mplug} with 51K reasoning instances paired with evidence pages, DocGenome12K \cite{hu2025mplug} with 12K scientific multi-page QA examples, and MMDU-45k \cite{liu2024mmdu} with 45K multi-turn multi-image dialogues. Long-context text instruction sets such as LongAlpaca \cite{chen2023longlora} (12K) and LongAlign \cite{bai2024longalign} (10K) extend instruction following to documents that exceed standard context windows. Beyond these shared collections, many systems generate synthetic page-grounded question-answer or trajectory data on demand for retrieval, reinforcement learning, and tool-use agent training \cite{cho2024m3docrag, jain2025simpledoc, wu2025molorag, xiong2026docr1, zhang2026docdancer}, with differentiation arising from the generation pipeline rather than the document substrate. These distributions are bespoke and rarely released in compatible form, which limits cross-model comparison.

\subsection{Benchmark Datasets}
Benchmarks have evolved from extractive multi-page DocVQA toward longer documents, multimodal evidence, and explicit grounding. Foundational answer-accuracy benchmarks include MP-DocVQA \cite{tito2023hierarchical} with 46K questions over 5,928 industrial documents averaging 8.3 pages, DUDE \cite{van2023document} with 41K questions across 5,019 cross-domain documents averaging 5.7 pages, and SlideVQA \cite{tanaka2023slidevqa} with 14K questions over 2,619 slide decks of 20 images each. PDF-VQA \cite{ding2023vqa} targets structural reasoning over 1,147 scientific PDFs, MMVQA \cite{ding2024mmvqa} releases 263K multipage multimodal retrieval annotations over 3,146 PubMed articles, and DuReader-vis \cite{qi2022dureadervis} extends open-domain document VQA to Chinese. A second wave stresses long-context multimodal reasoning. MMLongBench-Doc \cite{ma2024mmlongbench} contains 1{,}082 expert-annotated questions over 135 PDFs averaging 47.5 pages, with 33.7\% cross-page questions and 20.6\% deliberately unanswerable items. LongDocURL \cite{deng2025longdocurl} combines understanding, reasoning, and locating subtasks over 396 documents averaging 85.6 pages, M-LongDoc \cite{chia2025m} targets super-long documents averaging 210.8 pages, M3DocVQA \cite{cho2025m3docvqa} extends evaluation to 3,368 documents in an open-domain multi-document setting, DocBench \cite{zou2024docbench} covers 229 PDFs averaging 77.5 pages across five domains, and Needle-in-a-Multimodal-Haystack \cite{wang2024needle} probes fine-grained evidence localisation under extended multimodal context. Domain-specific evaluation remains relatively narrow, with DocGenome \cite{xia2024docgenome} as the main scientific-document benchmark and financial or legal cases entering largely through DUDE subsets. A third trend is evidence-aware evaluation that complements answer accuracy with retrieval or grounding metrics. UDA \cite{hui2024uda} frames document analysis as a RAG benchmark with 29K questions across 6 domains and reports retrieval together with answer scores, MMLongBench-Doc and LongDocURL report evidence-page F1 alongside answer F1, and recent agentic and reinforcement-learning systems track evidence-page recall directly \cite{xiong2026docr1, zhu2025doclens}. This shift reflects the recognition that a correct answer can be produced without correct evidence.

\section{Challenges and Future Directions}

\paragraph{Training Data Scarcity.}
Acquiring high-quality multi-page annotation is impractical at scale, since per-page labelling does not extend to documents averaging 8--20 pages. Many systems therefore rely on LVLM-driven synthetic question and trajectory generation \cite{PLACEHOLDER-synthetic-data-bias}. However, these pipelines often lack independent validation, and the synthetic distributions are bespoke and rarely released, undermining cross-model comparison. Multilingual coverage is a further blind spot, since the dominant benchmarks remain overwhelmingly English-centric \cite{PLACEHOLDER-multilingual-vrdu}. Future research should prioritise transparent synthetic-data protocols, shared multi-page corpora, and multilingual benchmarks covering non-English layouts and conventions.

\paragraph{Document-Scale Evidence.}
In practice, MP-VRDU systems must reconcile high-resolution per-page reading with document-scale evidence aggregation, an objective that exceeds any backbone's compute and context budget \cite{ma2024mmlongbench}. Every architectural family addresses this through some lossy compromise, each with characteristic failure modes such as small text, table cells, or figure annotations that are difficult to detect from the answer alone. Compression is also typically fixed at index time and independent of the question, so evidence may be discarded long before reasoning begins. Future work should explore query-conditional and modality-aware compression that retains evidence dynamically based on the question's evidential demands.

\paragraph{Effective Retrieval and Navigation.}
While retrieval has become central to MP-VRDU \cite{liu2024lost}, its recall upper-bounds answer accuracy, and broadening the retrieved set introduces noise and attention dilution. Existing approaches often exhibit brittle retrieval through chunking errors, OCR and parser propagation, modality mismatch, and per-passage scoring that ignores cross-reference and logical links. Iterative retrieval lifts this ceiling partially, yet inherits the recall of every module it calls. Future work should explore joint retriever-generator training, query-conditional graph construction, and retrieval that follows structural and logical edges rather than surface similarity alone.

\paragraph{Cross-Page Structural Reasoning.}
Within-page layout is largely inherited from single-page VRDU through 2D positional embeddings and spatial pretraining \cite{ding2025survey}, yet cross-page structure such as section hierarchy, page order, multi-page tables, and inter-page references remains the distinctive multi-page problem. Layout-as-objective demands supervision that does not scale, implicit pixel-based recovery depends on backbone capability and high-resolution input, and explicit parsed structure inherits parser fragility \cite{PLACEHOLDER-multipage-tables}. Future research should explore cheaper structural supervision, learned cross-page representations that survive compression, and end-to-end structure-aware retrieval.

\paragraph{Evaluation and Grounding.}
Current evaluation has three gaps. Answer-only accuracy can mask retrieval failure, allowing correct answers for the wrong reason, and although newer benchmarks score retrieval and page-localisation alongside answers \cite{hui2024uda}, the practice is not yet standardised. Agentic pipelines further produce stochastic trajectories whose tool calls and evidence selections vary across runs, complicating reproduction. Per-paper dataset curations also prevent like-for-like comparison across systems. Future work should explore trajectory-level evaluation, standardised retrieval-plus-grounding metrics, and controlled benchmarks that target cross-page reasoning specifically.
