\section{Introduction}
Visually-rich documents (VRDs) such as scientific papers, business reports, financial filings, contracts, and technical manuals are pervasive in real-world workflows, and understanding them is a long-standing problem at the intersection of NLP, computer vision, and document AI. Most prior work has focused on the single-page (SP) setting, where each input is a self-contained page, and the field has matured into a well-defined sub-area with established benchmarks and a clear taxonomy of OCR-based and OCR-free methods. However, real-world documents are rarely single-page: contracts span dozens of pages, scientific papers reference figures and tables many pages apart, and technical manuals contain multi-page tables and section hierarchies that no single-page view can capture. Multi-page visually rich document understanding (MP-VRDU) is not a simple extension of SP-VRDU, since total document length frequently exceeds the backbone's context window, evidence is sparsely distributed across pages, and document structure such as section hierarchies and cross-references cannot be recovered from per-token layout signals alone.

MP-VRDU has seen rapid growth in the past two years, with new architectures, training strategies, and inference-time methods emerging. Existing work spans architectural extensions of single-page document transformers~\cite{tito2023hierarchical, blau2024gram}, adaptations of pretrained multimodal LLMs~\cite{duan2025docopilot, hu2025mplug2}, retrieval-augmented pipelines~\cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag}, and increasingly agent-based systems~\cite{sun2025docagent, wang2025vidorag}, often combined with task-specific training or prompted reasoning loops~\cite{xiong2026docr1, han2025mdocagent}. However, this body of work has developed along largely parallel lines, with limited cross-family comparison and inconsistent terminology, and few works explicitly distinguish what is genuinely MP-distinctive from what is inherited from the single-page setting. As a result, the field lacks a unified view of the design choices that matter most in multi-page settings, motivating the need for systematic organisation.

\section{MP-VRDU Architecture Overview}
\label{sec:architecture}
Prior works have been organised primarily by OCR dependency, distinguishing OCR-based and OCR-free models~\citep{ding2025survey}. This axis is largely orthogonal to the central question in MP-VRDU, where systems differ chiefly in how cross-page evidence is selected, retained, and integrated across pages that exceed any single backbone's context window. Through this evidence-management lens, recent literature exhibits four recurring architectural trends: page-to-document encoding models, backbone-centric adaptation, retriever-generator pipelines, and agentic pipelines.

%Previous VRDU surveys \cite{ding2025survey} categorise models by OCR dependency, separating OCR-dependent models that consume text and layout boxes from OCR-free models that consume document images directly \cite{huang2022layoutlmv3}. This taxonomy is less informative in the multi-page setting, as OCR-using models often use extracted text as just one component in a multimodal pipeline \cite{sun2025docagent, wang2025vidorag}, while OCR-free models are often pretrained or aligned with OCR-derived supervision \cite{tanaka2025vdocrag, hu2024mplug}. The defining challenge in MP-VRDU is instead how a system manages document-scale evidence while bounded by context window limits. Answer-bearing evidence is typically sparse, distributed across pages, and multimodal in nature, so models must decide what information to retain, what to discard, and how the surviving information should interact across pages. These decisions are governed by architectural design, which determines how evidence is selected, preserved, aggregated, and exposed for reasoning. We therefore organise multi-page frameworks primarily by architectural trend, with OCR dependency as a secondary descriptor.

% \subsection{OCR Dependency}
% Earlier VRDU work typically separates OCR-dependent and OCR-free models according to whether they consume OCR tokens and layout boxes or read document images directly \cite{huang2022layoutlmv3, ding2025survey}. In MP-VRDU, this boundary becomes less binary, as OCR is often only one signal within a larger document-level system. It can support sparse retrieval, page grounding, bounding-box references, and textual verification, while visual encoders and VLMs handle image-based evidence \cite{han2025mdocagent, zhu2025laytokenllm, wang2025vidorag}. OCR remains valuable as structured text and layout coordinates provide compact representations for text-heavy or low-resolution pages; for example, LayTokenLLM projects OCR tokens, bounding boxes, and layout cues into the language-model space for efficient document reasoning \cite{zhu2025laytokenllm}. However, this compactness comes with OCR error propagation and parser dependence \cite{liao2025doclayllm}, and multi-page OCR-based systems still require retrieval, compression, or aggregation mechanisms to control document length \cite{hannan2025docslm, zhang2024cream}.\cite{hannan2025docslm, zhang2024cream}.

\subsection{Page-to-document Encoding Models}
% \textbf{Page-to-Document Encoding Models} encode each page through a shared document encoder including page level OCR text, layout, and visual features, then connect page representations through a document-level exchange mechanism such as summary tokens \cite{}, document-global tokens \cite{}, recurrent memory \cite{}, or sparse cross-page attention \cite{}. Cross-page interaction occur within the framework in a single forward pass, while page identity, local layout, and reading order remain explicit, providing a strong inductive bias for multi-page structure. Explicit cross-page modelling makes these models well suited to tasks where relevant evidence appears across pages within a coherent document structure, such as reports and business documents \cite{tito2023hierarchical, borchmann2025arctic}. Its limitations follow from the same design, as document-level representations may lose-fine grained evidence when page information is compressed or weakly connected \cite{blau2024gram, dong2024multi}. Moreover, computation grows with document length as all pages typically need to be encoded, making very long documents difficult to scale.
Page-to-document encoding models process each page through a shared layout-aware encoder such as LayoutLMv3~\cite{huang2022layoutlmv3}, that fuses OCR text, bounding-box layout, and visual features within the page, then connect per-page representations through cross-page attention~\cite{blau2024gram,borchmann2025arctic}, per-page summary tokens~\cite{tito2023hierarchical}, or recurrent state propagation~\cite{dong2024multi}. By preserving page boundaries and cross-page reading order in the architecture itself, this design provides a strong inductive bias for documents with regular structural templates such as multi-page forms and reports. However, every page must be jointly encoded in a single forward pass, and the page-to-document compression step discards fine-grained evidence as inputs grow~\cite{blau2024gram,dong2024multi}, pressures that subsequent families address by replacing the dedicated cross-page encoder with more flexible mechanisms.

% Rather than flattening all tokens and visual patches into one sequence, these models preserve page boundaries while connecting pages through summary tokens \cite{tito2023hierarchical}, global document tokens with local-global attention \cite{blau2024gram}, chunked sparse attention over long OCR streams \cite{borchmann2025arctic}, or recurrent memory passed across pages \cite{dong2024multi}. This design provides an inductive bias for multi-page understanding as evidence can be aggregated across pages without discarding page identity. However, cross-page reasoning is still bounded by the exchange mechanism, as summaries and memories can lose fine-grained evidence, sparse attention may miss distant dependencies, and computation generally grows with document length.

% This family uses a learned document encoder to separately encode individual pages, then introduces a document-level mechanism for information exchange across page representations. Hi-VT5 encodes each page independently and passes question-conditioned page tokens to the decoder, but its cross-page reasoning is delayed until after page-level compression \cite{tito2023hierarchical}. GRAM addresses this bottleneck by injecting document-global tokens into the encoder so pages can exchange information during representation learning, although its cost still increases with document length and optional compression can reduce answer quality \cite{blau2024gram}. RM-T5 instead carries recurrent memory tokens from page to page, making document aggregation more parameter-efficient, but fixed sequential memory can over-compress or forget earlier evidence \cite{dong2024multi}. Arctic-TILT pushes the same direction to much longer OCR-based inputs through layer-wise multimodal fusion and chunked sparse attention, but remains sensitive to OCR quality, visual layout complexity, and position bias in very long documents \cite{borchmann2025arctic}. These architectures preserve page structure and make document-level interaction explicit, but reasoning over many pages must still pass through compressed page summaries, memory states, or sparse attention patterns.

\subsection{Backbone-Centric Adaptation}
Backbone-centric adaptation removes the dedicated cross-page encoder entirely, instead processing the document inside a pretrained MLLM whose context window absorbs cross-page information directly. Adaptation strategies range from training only lightweight projectors~\cite{tanaka2024instructdoc} or visual-token compression modules~\cite{hu2025mplug2}, to fine-tuning the backbone itself through continued pretraining~\cite{duan2025docopilot}, instruction tuning, or reinforcement learning~\cite{xiong2026docr1}. This design avoids external retrieval or orchestration components and benefits directly from the MLLM's instruction-following and reasoning capabilities. However, the backbone's context length now caps total document size, fine-grained signals depend on the fidelity of token compression~\cite{jia2024leopard}, and document structure such as section hierarchy and figure-caption linkage must be recovered implicitly from pixels~\cite{hannan2025docslm}, limits that motivate moving evidence selection out of the backbone altogether.

% This family adapts a pretrained MLLM backbone to multi-page input by training projectors that map per-modality features into the backbone's token space \cite{tanaka2024instructdoc}, adaptors that enable parameter-efficient fine-tuning for document understanding \cite{zhu2025laytokenllm}, and compression modules that contain visual token growth on long inputs \cite{hu2025mplug2}. Some works further unfreeze the backbone for continued pretraining or instruction tuning on document corpora, trading parameter efficiency for stronger document-specific grounding \cite{duan2025docopilot}. These models inherit the instruction-following and multimodal reasoning of the foundation model and remain parameter-efficient to train, fitting tasks that combine document reading with open-ended reasoning and moderate-length documents where long-context tuning massively improves MLLM performance on multi-page documents \cite{hu2024mplug, yu2024texthawk2}. However, as the backbone must absorb document-scale evidence internally, performance degrades beyond moderate-length documents, leaving quality bound to context length and to how faithfully fine-grained text and visual signal survive token compression \cite{jia2024leopard}. Additionally, reasoning over visual elements depends entirely on the backbone's pretrained visual capabilities, and as most models in this family are OCR-free, reliable text recovery requires high-resolution input that aggressive downsampling readily compromises. Moreover, document structure such as section hierarchy and figure-caption linkage must likewise be recovered implicitly from pixels rather than supplied through layout tokens or a structural encoder \cite{hannan2025docslm, duan2025docopilot}.

% Backbone-adapted MP-VRDU methods extend pretrained MLLMs to support multi-page document understanding by modifying the model architecture, training strategy, or input representation. These methods aim to encode page images, OCR text, and layout information into a unified document-level representation. Early approaches mainly conducting continuing pretraining or instruction tuning on projectors or lightweight adapters to align document-specific visual and textual signals with pretrained MLLMs \cite{jia2024leopard, tanaka2024instructdoc}. Subsequent work places greater emphasis on efficient document representation, introducing visual token compression \cite{}, layout-aware encoding, streaming inference, or long-context training to reduce the computational cost of multi-page inputs \cite{hu2025mplug2, hannan2025docslm, yu2024texthawk2}. More recent studies further enhance document-level reasoning through large-scale multi-page supervision or evidence-guided reinforcement learning \cite{duan2025docopilot, xiong2026docr1}. Overall, this paradigm preserves the general instruction-following and multimodal reasoning capabilities of foundation models while adapting them to the structural and evidential demands of long documents. Its main limitation is that critical evidence must still be encoded and retained within the backbone, making performance highly dependent on context length, compression quality, OCR–vision alignment, and the preservation of fine-grained cross-page information.

\subsection{Retriever-Generator Pipelines}
Retriever-generator pipelines decouple evidence selection from answer generation: a retrieval module returns a compact subset of textual chunks, pages, or graph nodes, and a generator produces an answer conditioned on those units~\cite{cho2024m3docrag,tanaka2025vdocrag}. Because only the selected subset enters the generator, the family lifts the context-length and token-compression limits of backbone-centric models and supports documents that exceed any single backbone's context window. The retrieval and generation stages may be implemented by single modules or distributed across multiple specialised components~\cite{wang2024knowledge,duan2025m2rag,han2025mdocagent}, but the overall control flow remains a fixed pipeline executed once per query, which exposes the retrieved evidence as a partial audit trail. However, the design assumes that relevant evidence is rankable by query similarity in a single retrieval step, so evidence missed at retrieval is unrecoverable at generation, and questions that require aggregating multiple weakly relevant pages frequently fail~\cite{wu2025molorag}; performance is further bounded by upstream component quality, since chunking, OCR, and modality mismatches propagate directly into the answer, pressures that motivate replacing the fixed pipeline with adaptive, multi-step reasoning.

% Retriever-generator systems divide MP-VRDU into evidence selection and answer generation, where a retrieval module returns a compact subset of OCR chunks, pages, visual patches, multimodal elements, or graph nodes, and a generator answers conditioned only on those units \cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag}. Inference cost depends on the retrieved content rather than document length, allowing this architecture to scale to documents that exceed any current MLLM context window. Modular decomposition allows the retriever and generator to be swapped independently while retrieved evidence provides a partial audit trail for the answer. Furthermore, the same interface extends from a single document to a corpus, supporting multi-document question answering without significant architectural change. Structured domains with reliable retrieval anchors such as scientific papers and technical manuals benefit most \cite{shin2025multidocfusion}, and visually rich pages benefit further when page-level visual retrieval preserves figures, tables, and layout that OCR-only pipelines lose \cite{tanaka2025vdocrag, xie2024wukong}. However, evidence missed at retrieval is unrecoverable at generation, and broadening the retrieved set to compensate introduces noise and attention dilution. Additionally, performance depends on parser and embedding quality, while chunking errors, OCR mistakes, and retriever-generator modality mismatches propagate directly into the answer. The single-pass design also presupposes that relevant evidence is rankable by query similarity, which weakens when answers require aggregating pages that are not individually strongly relevant \cite{wang2024knowledge, wu2025molorag}.

% This family decomposes MP-VRDU into evidence selection and answer generation in which a retrieval module first identifies a compact subset of relevant document content, and a generator answers using only the selected evidence. Retrieved units may be OCR chunks, pages, visual patches, multmimodal elements, or graph nodes, depending on whether the document is represented as text, images, interleaved content, or a structured evidence graph. Retrieval can be performed through course-to-fine selection \cite{zhang2024cream}, adaptive visual retrieval \cite{li2025avir}, multimodal dense retrieval \cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag}, end-to-end sparse sampling over paragraphs and figures \cite{xie2024wukong}, graph-based prompting \cite{wang2024knowledge}, or logic-aware retrieval \cite{wu2025molorag}. This architecture scales well by preventing the generator from processing the full document, while the selected evidence provides partial interpretability. However, this introduces a retrieval bottleneck in which missed evidence is usually unrecoverable at generation time, while adding more retrieved context can introduce noise or attention dilution.

\subsection{Agentic Pipelines}
Agentic pipelines replace the fixed retrieval pipeline with iterative evidence acquisition: one or more LLM controllers issue successive tool calls, for retrieval, navigation, cropping, or OCR, and each subsequent decision is conditioned on the outcomes of earlier observations~\cite{zheng2026docvstar,sun2025docagent}. Roles such as inspection, synthesis, and adjudication may be distributed across heterogeneous backbones~\cite{yu2025mact,wang2025vidorag}, but the defining property is that the trajectory itself adapts to question difficulty rather than following a fixed control flow; multi-component systems with predetermined execution order, even when described as ``agents'', remain within the retriever-generator family. By lifting the single-retrieval-step bottleneck of retriever-generator systems, this design allows compute to scale with question difficulty rather than document length and exposes the trajectory as an inspectable audit trail. However, the variable-length action sequence makes inference cost difficult to bound before execution, controllers remain sensitive to prompt phrasing and may commit early to wrong evidence with silent failure, reproducibility is complicated by stochastic generation, and the system inherits the recall ceiling of every retriever, parser, or OCR module it calls.
% Recent surveys \cite{plaat2025agentic, wang2024survey} characterise agentic LLM systems through their capacity to reason, act, and interact with an external environment beyond a single forward pass. Following this framing, we categorise MP-VRDU systems that perform multi-pass evidence acquisition or distributes work across specialised agents as agentic, while reserving the retriever-generator family for single-pass models. Agentic systems thus treat MP-VRDU as an iterative evidence-seeking process, where one or more controller MLLMs decide what to inspect next from intermediate observations and issue tool calls for retrieval, navigation, cropping, or OCR. Roles such as retrieval, inspection, synthesis, and adjudication may be distributed across agents, with each agent backed by a different backbone model selected for its subtask \cite{han2025mdocagent} rather than tied to a single generator in retriever-generator systems. This design lifts the single-pass bottleneck, allows compute to scale with question difficulty rather than document length, and the trajectory itself becomes an inspectable audit trail. This suits questions whose evidence is distributed across pages or modalities and multi-hop questions that need query reformulation between steps \cite{wang2025vidorag, sun2025docagent}, and visually heterogeneous documents whose text, tables, and figures benefit from tool-specific inspection \cite{han2025mdocagent, zhu2025doclens}. However, adaptability in compute is difficult to bound before inference, and action policies and selection criteria remain sensitive to prompt phrasing, with silent failure when the controller commits early to wrong evidence. Moreover, reproducibility is complicated by stochastic generation and the absence of a single attributable forward pass, and the system inherits the recall ceiling of every retriever, parser, or OCR module it calls.

% This family treats MP-VRDU as an iterative evidence seeking process rather than a fixed retrieve-then-generate pass. In this survey, this category refers to systems that use multi-agent decomposition, tool use, memory, or open-ended control to navigate long documents, inspect evidence, refine queries, and revise answers. Some pipelines assign retrieval, visual inspection, modal fusion, judgement and answer synthesis across specialised agents \cite{han2025mdocagent, duan2025m2rag, yu2025mact}, or use tool-augment and dynamic iterative workflows to navigate documents, inspect evidence, maintain intermediate states, and revise answers \cite{sun2025docagent, zhu2025doclens, zheng2026docvstar, wang2025vidorag}. This design is effective when evidence is scattered, visually fine-grained, or missed by a single retrieval step, allowing the model to gather additional context before answer generation. However, this introduces higher latency, repeated model calls, dependence on external parsing or tool quality, and greater difficulty in reproducing and evaluating the full decision process.

\section{Multi-Page Multimodal Representation}
While §\ref{sec:architecture} categorises MP-VRDU systems by where cross-page evidence is processed, this section analyses the problem from a modality perspective. Compared with single-page VRDU, multi-page settings introduce distinct challenges for text, vision, and document structure: text may exceed the backbone context window and appear sparsely across pages; visual processing becomes expensive as both page number and resolution increase; and document-level structures such as section hierarchies, multi-page tables, and cross-references cannot be captured by single-page layout signals alone. We therefore discuss text, visual, and structural modelling in turn (§\ref{sec:modality-text}, §\ref{sec:modality-visual}, §\ref{sec:modality-structure}), focusing on the issues specific to MP-VRDU rather than those already covered in single-page surveys~\cite{ding2025survey}. Modality fusion is treated as a complementary cross-cutting issue in Appendix~\ref{app:fusion}.
% MP-VRDU models must model text, visual elements, and layout over multiple pages. Hierarchical models usually encode modalities locally within each page before document-level aggregation. Backbone-centric MLLMs project or compress multimodal page evidence into the backbone context. Retrieval-generator and agentic pipeline models convert modalities into searchable or navigable evidence units.

\subsection{Text Modality}
\label{sec:modality-text}
In SP-VRDU, text is typically processed by inserting OCR-extracted strings into the LLM prompt, encoding text jointly with layout and visual features through an auxiliary multimodal encoder, or training the model to read text directly from page pixels~\cite{ding2025survey}. Because a page fits entirely within the backbone's input, the model reads it once and locates the answer within the same pass. MP-VRDU faces two challenges: the document often exceeds the backbone's context window, and even when the full document fits, the answer occupies only a small fraction of a long input, where attention is diluted and key statements are easily lost in the middle~\cite{liu2024lost}.

Three patterns have emerged for handling text under these challenges. \textbf{Per-page aggregation} encodes each page in detail and combines per-page representations into compact cross-page summaries through attention or memory tokens, as in Hi-VT5~\cite{tito2023hierarchical}, GRAM~\cite{blau2024gram}, and Arctic-TILT~\cite{borchmann2025arctic}. \textbf{Whole-document compression} retains all pages within a single backbone context by aggressively reducing per-page text tokens through resamplers, layout-aware compressors, or token-reduction modules, as in mPLUG-DocOwl2~\cite{hu2025mplug2} and Texthawk2~\cite{yu2024texthawk2}. \textbf{Retrieve-then-read} abandons full-document encoding entirely, indexing text at chunk or page level and passing only a retrieved subset to the reader, as in CREAM~\cite{zhang2024cream}, MultiDocFusion~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion}, DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent}, and MDocAgent~\cite{han2025mdocagent}; agentic variants extend this into multiple selection-reading rounds. Each pattern carries a characteristic risk: per-page aggregation discards token-level detail in the summary step, whole-document compression sacrifices fine-grained reading on individual pages, and retrieve-then-read makes evidence missed at retrieval unrecoverable at the reading stage. The last two routes also depend on document-level structure to decide which pages or sections to read in detail, which we discuss in §\ref{sec:modality-structure}.

% Text is the most information-dense modality in MP-VRDU, carrying the bulk of a document's explicit semantic content in a compact, searchable form, obtained either through OCR or recovered directly from page pixels by an LVLM. Extracted text from OCR supports cheap indexing and retrieval over documents that exceed context windows, natural answer-passage attribution, and bridges to visual elements via captions and references. However, this comes at the cost of OCR error propagation, fragile multi-column and table reading order, and the loss of chart, layout, and handwriting semantics. Page-to-document transformers typically rely on this acquisition method, treating OCR-extracted text as the primary per-page input, encoded jointly with layout and visual embeddings and exchanged across pages through document-level aggregation \cite{tito2023hierarchical, blau2024gram, borchmann2025arctic}. In contrast, pixel-recovered text avoids OCR dependence but demands high-resolution input and extensive text-recognition pretraining, both of which scale poorly with document length. Backbone-centric MLLMs typically rely on this method, reading text through the backbone's internal vision capabilities, applied to whole-document context in long-context variants \cite{hu2025mplug2, yu2024texthawk2} and selectively to retrieved regions in others \cite{zheng2026docvstar, jain2025simpledoc}. Retriever-generators use text as a searchable index over chunks, pages, or summaries and as evidence passed to the generator alongside or in place of visual inputs \cite{zhang2024cream, shin2025multidocfusion}, while agentic pipelines additionally use it as a navigation layer where outlines and section searches direct later visual inspection \cite{han2025mdocagent, sun2025docagent, wang2025vidorag}.

Work tokens are paired with bounding boxes, page identifiers, visual patches, and query-conditioned summary or memory tokens. This is so that each page can be encoded locally before document-level aggregation \cite{tito2023hierarchical, dong2024multi}. Later variants add document-global tokens, sparse attention, or layer-wise multimodal fusion to let OCR-based page representations exchange information across pages without fully concatenating all tokens \cite{blau2024gram, borchmann2025arctic}. In this family, OCR text provides the semantic backbone of the page, while layout and visual features preserve grounding.

Thus, text is best viewed as a searchable scaffold for multimodal evidence gathering, not a stand-alone document representation.

\subsection{Visual Modality}
\label{sec:modality-visual}
In SP-VRDU, visual content is typically encoded by feeding image patches through a pretrained vision encoder, optionally at multiple resolutions or with shape-adaptive cropping, and aligned with text and layout features through projectors or adaptors~\cite{ding2025survey}. A single page can be encoded at the resolution required to read fine text, table cells, and chart elements within the model's input. MP-VRDU faces a sharper tension: visual encoding must remain fine-grained enough to recover charts, tables, and handwritten content that OCR cannot, yet scalable enough to apply across documents spanning tens or hundreds of pages. 

Three patterns have emerged for managing this tension. \textbf{Aggressive token compression} keeps every page available in context but reduces per-page visual tokens through resamplers or convolutional reducers, as in mPLUG-DocOwl2~\cite{hu2025mplug2} and Texthawk2~\cite{yu2024texthawk2}; this preserves coverage at the cost of per-page detail. \textbf{Selective high-resolution reading} restricts expensive encoding to a small subset of pages surfaced by retrieval or controller-driven inspection, sending those high-fidelity tokens into the reader while leaving other pages unread, as in Doc-V*~\cite{zheng2026docvstar} and SimpleDoc~\cite{jain2025simpledoc}. \textbf{Retrieval-only visual embeddings} decouple encoding from reading altogether: page images are encoded purely to produce retrieval embeddings, and detailed reading of selected pages is handed to a separate text or vision module, as in M3DocRAG~\cite{cho2024m3docrag} and VDocRAG~\cite{tanaka2025vdocrag}. Each pattern carries a characteristic risk: aggressive compression loses fine text and small visual elements, selective high-resolution reading inherits the recall ceiling of whichever tool selects the pages, and retrieval-only visual embeddings may rank layout-similar pages without confirming the answer-bearing region is present.
% Visual elements in MP-VRDU range from text-bearing structures such as dense paargraphs and tables to non-textual evidence such as charts, figures, and handwriting that OCR cannot recover. Visual token cost scales with both page count and per-page resolution, making full document high-resolution encoding infeasible at realistic document length. This forces every architecture to trade page coverage against reading fidelity within a fixed token budget. Some models \cite{hu2025mplug2, zheng2026docvstar, jain2025simpledoc} keep all pages available at low resolution to preserve document-scale visual evidence at low token cost while relying on OCR or parsed text to supply finer reading where the down-sampled image is insufficient. Others \cite{hu2025mplug2, guo2026end} sidestep this tradeoff through selective high-resolution reading, restricting expensive visual encoding to pages or regions surfaced by retrieval, navigation, or controller driven inspection. Retriever-generator systems \cite{cho2024m3docrag, tanaka2025vdocrag, li2025avir} take a different route, often use visual page embeddings purely as a retrieval signal rather than as a reading input. These approaches are not mutually exclusive, and many systems \cite{zheng2026docvstar, jain2025simpledoc} pair low-resolution thumbnails for navigation with selective high-resolution inspection of pages a retriever or tool-call surfaces. Each strategy, however, carries a characteristic failure mode. Low-resolution context loses fine text and small visual elements, selective high-resolution reading inherits the recall ceiling of whichever tool chooses what to read, and visual retrieval can rank pages whose layout looks relevant without confirming the answer-bearing region is present. Compression and tokenisation choices further determine how reliably embedded text, table cells, and figure annotations survive into the model input.

\subsection{Structure Modality}
\label{sec:modality-structure}
In SP-VRDU, structural information is mainly captured as within-page layout, including 2D positional encodings, bounding-box coordinates, and reading-order signals attached to individual tokens. It is incorporated through layout-aware pretraining objectives, layout-as-prompt formulations, or implicit recovery from page pixels by an OCR-free MLLM. These mechanisms remain valid in MP-VRDU but only describe the geometry within each page. The structural problem distinctive to MP-VRDU is \textit{cross-page} structure: section hierarchies that span multiple pages, information referenced or continued across page boundaries, and document-level relationships such as figure-to-text or table-to-caption linkages that no longer fit within a single page's view. Per-token bounding boxes do not encode such relationships, and large-scale supervision for document-scale structure is expensive to obtain. Some approaches address this by training the backbone to predict cross-page structure end-to-end through multi-page parsing or document-level pretraining objectives~\cite{hu2025mplug2, duan2025docopilot}, but pretraining data at this scale remains scarce. 

The dominant route in current MP-VRDU is therefore to expose parsed structural information to the model directly. Three patterns have emerged. \textbf{Structure-aware chunking} uses a parser to extract section trees and heading hierarchies, then segments documents along semantic boundaries so retrieved units respect section scope rather than sliding-window cuts, as in MultiDocFusion~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion}. \textbf{Hierarchical retrieval indices} route queries first to coarse units such as sections or chapters and then to fine-grained chunks within the selected branch, reducing irrelevant pages while preserving cross-page context, as in MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} and MLDocRAG~\cite{zhang2026mldocrag}. \textbf{Graph-based traversal} converts headings, entities, and cross-references into a knowledge graph, allowing controllers to follow typed edges across pages rather than relying on dense similarity alone, as in KGP~\cite{wang2024knowledge}. All three depend on parser quality: errors in heading detection, table boundary segmentation, or reading-order extraction propagate directly into every downstream retrieval and navigation step.

\section{Training Strategies}
\label{sec:training}
Training strategies in MP-VRDU are closely tied to how each architecture controls the cost and complexity of multi-page document understanding. While the previous section describe how models represent documents and combine text, visual, and layout evidence, this section focuses on how these capabilities are learned.

\subsection{Parameter Efficienct Tuning}
A common strategy is to reuse strong pretrained LLMs or LVLMs and update only a small number of task-specific components. Rather than training vision encoders or language backbones end-to-end, these methods freeze most pretrained parameters and introduce lightweight modules such as LoRA, projectors, adapters, layout tokenizers, or task-specific heads. 
LayTokenLLM~\cite{zhu2025laytokenllm} exemplifies this by keeping the LLM backbone frozen and introducing a lightweight layout tokenization module together with LoRA-based adaptation. Rather than modifying the architecture, it injects layout information into the input sequence using a compressed representation and a specialized positional encoding scheme that avoids additional context overhead, enabling efficient scaling to long multi-page documents. As an alternative, some work adopts retrieval-augmented strategies, which avoid directly modeling entire documents by selecting only the most relevant content for downstream reasoning. CREAM~\cite{zhang2024cream} introduces a coarse-to-fine retrieval framework. It first retrieves candidate text chunks based on embedding similarity, and then applies multi-round LLM-based re-ranking to identify the most relevant evidence. Extending this idea, MoLoRAG~\cite{wu2025molorag} incorporates logical reasoning into retrieval by constructing a graph over document pages and performing multi-hop traversal, enabling the model to capture both semantic and logical relevance across pages.

A representative example is DocSLM~\cite{hannan2025docslm}, which adopts multi-stage curriculum. Beyond architectural design, another key challenge lies in effective retrieval mechanisms. \section{Inference-Time Adaptation}
\label{sec:inference-time}
Inference-time adaptation refers to strategies that improve MP-VRDU performance through changes to the inference pipeline rather than to model parameters. A frozen LLM/MLLM backbone is paired with off-the-shelf retrievers, structural parsers, prompted reasoning loops, or multi-agent orchestrations, none of which require gradient updates on the backbone itself. This makes inference-time adaptation particularly suited to MP-VRDU settings where supervised data is scarce and expensive to train with, where backbones must remain exchangeable across systems, or where rapid integration with new document types is needed without retraining. We organise these strategies along the inference pipeline: evidence selection through retrieval and navigation (§\ref{sec:tf-retrieval}), inference-time reasoning strategies (§\ref{sec:tf-reasoning}), and coordination across multiple frozen components (§\ref{sec:agent-pipeline}).

Strategies include offline document-side preparation, such as parsing the document into a graph, tree, or outline, as well as online inference-side orchestrations through prompting, tool use, retrieval refinement, and multi-agent coordination. Compared to supervised training, retrieval tuning, and reinforcement learning, training-free strategies remain less systematically explored despite their growing prevalence in agentic and retrieval-based systems for multi-page document understanding.

\subsection{Retrieval and Navigation}
\label{sec:tf-retrieval}
Inference-time retrieval selects a compact subset of pages, chunks, or graph nodes for the reader using off-the-shelf encoders, structural parsers, or LLM-based rerankers, without fine-tuning the retriever on document data. Retrieval quality bounds every downstream reasoning step, so the choice of signal dominates system behaviour on long documents. Two broad families have emerged, distinguished by whether passages are scored in isolation or navigated through explicit relations.

\paragraph{Similarity-based Retrieval.}
Similarity-based methods score each passage against the query independently, using sparse lexical signals, dense embeddings, or combinations of both. The dominant dense signal in MP-VRDU is vision-language late-interaction encoding (ColPali, ColBERT-v2, ColQwen), which produces token-level query vectors and patch-level page embeddings and scores their maximum alignment, preserving fine-grained match between query terms and specific page regions. M3DocRAG~\cite{cho2024m3docrag} retrieves at chunk and page granularity with this signal alone; AVIR~\cite{li2025avir} adds an adaptive selector that adjusts the number of returned pages per query. Joint variants combine signals at the reranking stage: CREAM~\cite{zhang2024cream} pairs BGE dense retrieval with iterative LLM-based reranking, SimpleDoc~\cite{jain2025simpledoc} combines ColPali embeddings with LLM-generated summary reranking under persistent working memory, and MDocAgent~\cite{han2025mdocagent} runs parallel text and image retrieval tracks whose outputs are aggregated downstream. Independent scoring handles paraphrase and cross-modal queries well, but cannot follow cross-page references or multi-hop dependencies that no single passage carries.

\paragraph{Structure-based Retrieval.}
Structure-based methods exploit a parsed representation of the document to make passage relations first-class objects. Tree-based variants such as MultiDocFusion~\cite{shin2025multidocfusion} and DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} extract section hierarchies and outlines and group passages along semantic boundaries so retrieved chunks preserve parent context, while MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} extends this by routing queries through dual in-page and cross-page indices. Graph-based variants treat headings, entities, and cross-references as graph nodes with typed edges, allowing controllers to traverse from one passage to related ones rather than relying on similarity alone: KGP~\cite{wang2024knowledge} uses an LLM as a traverser over a passage-and-structure graph, while MoLoRAG~\cite{wu2025molorag} and MLDocRAG~\cite{zhang2026mldocrag} extend this with logical relevance scoring or query-conditioned graph construction. Structure-based methods recover references that similarity scoring misses, at the cost of dependence on parser quality and traversal cost that grows with the hop count.

\subsection{Inference-Time Reasoning Strategies}
\label{sec:tf-reasoning}
Inference-time reasoning strategies govern how a frozen LLM or VLM accesses, updates, and reasons over evidence during decoding, without modifying model parameters. In MP-VRDU, these strategies can be broadly categorised by whether evidence acquisition remains static or becomes dynamically interactive during inference. \textbf{Closed-buffer reasoning} operates over a fixed evidence set assembled before generation begins, whereas \textbf{tool-interactive reasoning} allows the model to iteratively acquire additional evidence through retrieval or page-level actions during the reasoning process.

\paragraph{Closed-buffer Reasoning.}
Closed-buffer reasoning performs multi-step reasoning over a fixed evidence buffer assembled by a prior retrieval step or by a compressed document representation. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting~\cite{wei2022chain} is the canonical instantiation, eliciting intermediate reasoning steps that integrate textual, tabular, and visual evidence before producing the final answer. MHier-RAG~\cite{gong2025mhier} applies structured CoT prompts over hierarchical retrieval outputs, while SimpleDoc~\cite{jain2025simpledoc} adds self-refinement and self-verification prompts that emit revised queries when the assembled evidence is judged insufficient. Closed-buffer reasoning is fundamentally bounded by retrieval completeness: evidence absent from the buffer cannot be recovered by subsequent reasoning or verification.

\paragraph{Tool-interactive Reasoning.}
Tool-interactive reasoning interleaves reasoning and evidence acquisition during inference, allowing newly observed pages to guide subsequent reasoning steps. ReAct prompting~\cite{yao2022react} is the canonical instantiation, alternating intermediate reasoning traces with tool calls whose outputs feed the next reasoning stage. Doc-React~\cite{wu2025docreact} instantiates this with flat retrieval, sub-query refinement, and entropy-based stopping, while outline-driven variants combine ReAct with the structured navigation discussed in §\ref{sec:tf-retrieval}. Adaptive evidence acquisition improves localisation and supports more exploratory multi-page reasoning, but introduces tool latency that grows with rounds, sensitivity to prompt design and to early action choices that subsequent steps cannot recover from, and attention dilution within fetched pages at their fixed reading resolution.

\subsection{Agentic Orchestration}
\label{sec:agent-orchestration}
Retriever-generator and agentic pipelines often distribute a single MP-VRDU question across multiple specialised LLM or VLM calls, each handling a particular modality, reasoning step, or verification role. The frozen backbones are not modified; coordination is achieved through prompt design and call ordering. The two families correspond directly to whether the trajectory of calls is fixed at design time or adapts to the question at inference: retriever-generator pipelines run a trajectory fixed in advance regardless of how many agents they invoke, while agentic pipelines treat the call sequence itself as a variable.

\paragraph{Fixed-Trajectory Pipelines.}
Fixed-trajectory pipelines follow a control flow set in advance: which calls are made, in what order, and how their outputs are combined are all determined at design time. Modality-partitioned variants dispatch readers by input type and aggregate their outputs through a final synthesis step: MDocAgent~\cite{han2025mdocagent} runs five parallel agents fused by a summariser, while M2RAG~\cite{duan2025m2rag} splits work between a text filter, a visual extractor, and a modal fuser that falls back to one modality when the other reports no information. Such pipelines improve coverage relative to single-agent baselines, but a wrong choice of routing or ordering cannot be revised mid-execution.

\paragraph{Adaptive-Trajectory Pipelines.}
Adaptive-trajectory pipelines treat the call sequence itself as a variable: which agents are invoked and how many rounds the loop runs are decided per question rather than fixed in advance. Loop based variants such as ViDoRAG~\cite{wang2025vidorag} cycles a seeker that retrieves coarse candidates, an inspector that reviews them at fine resolution, and an answerer that performs a consistency check, with a Gaussian mixture model setting per-query K bounds from the similarity distribution rather than using a static top-K. Trained variants such as MACT~\cite{yu2025mact} extend this with a reinforcement-learned orchestration policy (§\ref{sec:training}). Proposer-verifier variants insert an explicit verification stage between candidate generation and final answer: DocLens~\cite{zhu2025doclens} samples multiple candidates and routes them through an adjudicator that compares reasoning paths, while DocAgent~\cite{sun2025docagent} pairs an actor with a reviewer that cross-checks through complementary tools and writes disagreements into a shared reflection memory. Adaptive-trajectory pipelines improve the accuracy-latency trade-off, but inference cost remains hard to bound before execution, scaling jointly with the number of calls involved.

\section{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}
Datasets in MP-VRDU span three roles. Pretraining historically relied on single-page archives such as IIT-CDIP \cite{lewis2006iitcdip} and OCR-IDL \cite{biten2022ocridl}, with genuinely multi-page corpora only recently emerging through DocStruct4M's mixed single- and multi-page pretraining \cite{hu2024mplug} and the purely multi-page MP-DocStruct1M, Doc-750K, and scientific PaperPDF \cite{hu2025mplug2, duan2025docopilot, xie2024wukong}. Fine-tuning has shifted toward bespoke synthetic generation per model, with strong LVLMs writing question--answer or trajectory data over scraped PDFs since per-page annotation does not scale to long documents \cite{tanaka2024instructdoc, jain2025simpledoc, xiong2026docr1, zhang2026docdancer}. Benchmarks have evolved from mid-length extractive evaluation on MP-DocVQA, DUDE, and SlideVQA \cite{tito2023hierarchical, van2023dude, tanaka2023slidevqa} to long-context multimodal evaluation on MMLongBench-Doc, LongDocURL, and DocBench \cite{ma2024mmlongbench, deng2025longdocurl, zou2024docbench}, with retrieval and page-localisation increasingly scored alongside answer accuracy \cite{hui2024uda, xiong2026docr1, zhu2025doclens}. Five benchmarks (MP-DocVQA, MMLongBench-Doc, DUDE, DocVQA, and InfographicVQA) appear in roughly half or more of the surveyed papers and constitute the de-facto axes for cross-model comparison. \section{Challenges and Future Direction}
\label{challenges}

\paragraph{Training and Supervision Scarcity.}
Acquiring high-quality multi-page annotation is impractical at scale, as per-page labelling does not extend to documents averaging as little as 8-20 pages \cite{}. Many systems therefore rely on LVLM-driven synthetic question and trajectory generation \cite{}. However, these pipelines often lack independent validation, and these datasets are bespoke are rarely released to the public. Multilingual coverage is a further blind spot, as dominant benchmarks remain overwhelmingly English-centric \cite{}. Future research should prioritise transparent synthetic-data protocols, shared multi-page corpora, and multilingual benchmarks covering non-english layouts and conventions.

\paragraph{Evaluation Integrity.}
The shift toward LLM-synthesised training data has compromised evaluation integrity in a structural way. MP-DocVQA appears in 15 of the 36 surveyed papers' fine-tuning mixes and 17 of their evaluation suites, with DUDE and DocVQA showing similar overlap, so performance reported as zero-shot often includes the test corpus in its training history. Compounding this, of the 12 papers that introduce a synthesised training corpus, 9 source their documents from existing evaluation benchmarks, producing a circular loop in which a model is trained on a strong LVLM's behaviour over benchmark PDFs and then evaluated against the same PDFs under metrics often judged by the same LVLM. No paper in the corpus reports human-rated synthetic-data quality, document-hash deduplication against benchmark test pools, or backbone-level contamination audits. Future work should adopt held-out closed-test benchmarks, mandatory contamination declarations covering pretraining, fine-tuning, and backbone leakage, and generator-evaluator separation when synthetic data is involved.

\paragraph{Document-Scale Evidence.}
In practice, MP-VRDU models \textbf{must} reconcile high-resolution per-page reading with document-scale evidence aggregation, an objective that exceeds any MLLM's compute and context budget \cite{ma2024mmlongbench}. Every architectural family addresses this through some lossy compromise, silently dropping fine-grained content such as small text, table cells, and figure annotations. Compression is also typically fixed at index time and independent of the question, so evidence may be discarded long before reasoning begins. Future work should explore query-conditional and modality-aware compression that retains evidence dynamically based on the question's evidential demands.

\paragraph{Effective Retrieval and Navigation.}
While retrieval has become central to MP-VRDU \cite{liu2024lost}, its performance also define the upper bounds of answer accuracy, while broadening the retrieved set introduces noise and attention dilution. Existing approaches often exhibit brittle retrieval through chunking errors, OCR and parser propagation, modality mismatch, and per-passage scoring that ignores cross-reference and logical links. Iterative retrieval lifts this ceiling partially, yet inherits the recall of every tool/module it calls. Future work should iunno.

\paragraph{Cross-Page Structural Reasoning.}
Within-page layout is largely inherited from single-page VRDU through 2D positional embeddings and spatial pretraining \cite{ding2025survey}, yet cross-page structure such as section hierarchy, page order, multi-page tables, and inter-page references remains the distinctive multi-page problem. Layout-as-objective demands supervision that does not scale, implicit pixel-based recovery depends on backbone capability and high-resolution input, and explicit parsed structure inherits parser fragility \cite{}. Future research should explore cheaper structural supervision, learned cross-page representations that survive compression, and end-to-end structure-aware retrieval.

\paragraph{Evaluation, Grounding, and Reproducibility}
Current evaluation has 3 gaps. Answer-only accuracy can mask retrieval failure, allow correct answer for the wrong reason, and although newer benchmarks score retrieval and page-localisation alongside answers \cite{hui2024uda}, the practice is not yet standardised. Agentic pipelines further produce stochastic trajectories whose tool calls and evidence selection vary across runs, complication reproducibility. Per-paper dataset curations also prevent comparison across systems. Future work should explore trajectory-level evaluation and standardised retrieval combined with grounding metrics.

\section*{Limitations}
This survey covers approximately 36 multi-page VRDU models published before May 2026 and is bounded by the rapid pace of the field, with new architectures, training-free methods, and benchmarks appearing during preparation that may not be reflected here. Selection prioritises representative coverage of architectural and inference-time strategies over exhaustive enumeration. Cross-model performance comparisons are inherently limited since training distributions, benchmark coverage, and evaluation metrics are bespoke per system, and quantitative claims should be interpreted as indicative rather than definitive.

\subsection{Multimodal Fusion}
\label{app:fusion}
General MLLM-based VRDU surveys cover common fusion mechanisms such as neural fusion, target-oriented fusion, and prompt-based fusion \cite{ding2025survey}. The multi-page setting adds a further axis: at which point in the pipeline modalities are combined, and how selectively that combination is applied across pages. Full text-vision-layout fusion across an entire document is rarely computationally feasible, so the surveyed systems fall into three approximate stages of fusion, catalogued below.

\paragraph{Early Fusion.}
Text, visual, and layout features are combined before or during document encoding. In page-to-document transformers, fusion is local: OCR tokens, bounding boxes, visual patches, and question tokens are combined within each page and then aggregated through page summaries \cite{tito2023hierarchical}, document-level tokens \cite{blau2024gram}, recurrent memory \cite{dong2024multi}, or sparse text-image-layout attention \cite{borchmann2025arctic}. Backbone-centric MLLMs apply early fusion at projection time, mapping visual or multimodal features into the LLM token space through adapters, projectors, or document-former modules \cite{tanaka2024instructdoc, duan2025docopilot, jia2024leopard}. The pattern preserves fine-grained multimodal alignment at the cost of joint computation over many pages.

\paragraph{Late Fusion.}
Modalities are kept separate during retrieval or inspection and combined only after candidate evidence has been selected. Retriever-generator systems identify relevant passages via text chunks before visual generation \cite{zhang2024cream}, perform page-image retrieval that keeps multimodal evidence fused at the page level \cite{cho2024m3docrag}, or combine parsed text, visual descriptions, parent pages, and cross-page summaries only for retrieved evidence \cite{gong2025mhier}. Agentic pipelines extend the pattern by inspecting text, image, table, and page-level evidence separately before final synthesis or adjudication \cite{han2025mdocagent, duan2025m2rag, zhu2025doclens}. The pattern scales to long documents but depends on retrieval and routing quality.

% Not that great, could omit entirely.
\paragraph{Staged Fusion.}
Local alignment is combined with selective document-level reasoning. Modalities are fused within pages or regions, then compressed, retrieved, or stored selectively before a final cross-page fusion step. Some hierarchical and recurrent models approximate the pattern by producing page-level summaries or memories prior to document-level aggregation \cite{tito2023hierarchical, dong2024multi}, and compact long-document models reduce local multimodal evidence into fixed-size page representations \cite{hannan2025docslm}. Fully developed staged fusion is rare in the surveyed corpus.

\subsection{Model Survey by OCR Dependency}
\label{app:ocr-survey}
Table~\ref{tab:model_survey_ocr} reorganises the surveyed systems along the OCR-dependency axis to complement the architectural grouping in Table~\ref{tab:model_survey_arch}. OCR-using systems dominate page-to-document transformers, which require explicit text tokens as input, and remain common across retriever-generator pipelines that index parsed text alongside page images. OCR-free systems concentrate in backbone-centric MLLMs that rely on the backbone's internal vision capabilities, and in agentic pipelines orchestrating frozen LVLMs. Modality coverage tracks this split, since OCR-using systems typically expose text, vision, and layout while OCR-free systems usually operate over visual tokens alone. Asterisked entries denote hybrid cases in which OCR is incorporated as an auxiliary framework component or appears only during training. Read together with Table~\ref{tab:model_survey_arch}, we demonstrate that OCR dependency is largely orthogonal to architectural family rather than a defining feature, as discuessed in \S\ref{sec:architecture}.

\subsection{Reported Performance by Architecture}
\label{app:performance}
Table~\ref{tab:model_performance_arch} summarises reported scores on the five most-adopted multi-page benchmarks, grouped by architectural family. The matrix is sparse, as most papers report on only two or three of the five benchmarks, and metric conventions diverge across MP-DocVQA (ANLS), DUDE (ANLS), LongDocURL (Accuracy), MMLongBench-Doc (Accuracy and F1), and SlideVQA (EM and F1). Page-to-document transformers concentrate on mid-length benchmarks where the dataset matches the architectural inductive bias, with limited coverage of long-document tasks. Backbone-centric MLLMs span a wider range, with newer entries such as DocR1 and Docopilot reporting on long-document benchmarks as well. Retriever-generator and agentic systems dominate the long-document frontier, since selective evidence access is the design lever that makes 50+ page evaluation tractable. Cross-family comparison should account for backbone scale and proprietary LLM dependency, as agentic pipelines often pair a closed-source generator with a retrieval module while page-to-document and backbone-centric families typically report numbers under open-source weights. The reported scores should be read alongside Table~\ref{tab:model_training_status}, which clarifies whether a number reflects supervised fine-tuning on the benchmark or zero-shot evaluation.

\subsection{Training Status by Component}
\label{app:training-status}
Table~\ref{tab:model_training_status} records which components are trained, frozen, or not applicable in each surveyed system, broken down by vision encoder, LLM backbone, and adaptor across pretraining, instruction-tuning, and fine-tuning stages. Three patterns emerge. End-to-end systems train multiple components, often jointly, and account for most page-to-document transformers and several backbone-centric MLLMs that absorb document evidence into the backbone. Partially trained systems freeze the heavy backbone and update only a light adaptor or projection layer, a pattern common to retriever-generator pipelines and to retrieval-augmented backbone variants, since parameter-efficient adaptation allows reuse of strong pretrained vision-language priors. Training-free systems perform no parameter updates at all, the dominant pattern in agentic pipelines that orchestrate frozen LVLMs through prompting, tool use, and multi-agent decomposition. The training-status view also clarifies why some numbers in Table~\ref{tab:model_performance_arch} are not directly comparable, as systems trained on a benchmark naturally outperform training-free systems evaluated zero-shot on the same suite. Empty rows for training-free entries reflect this orchestration-only design rather than missing data.

\subsection{Model Components Details}
\label{app:components}
Table~\ref{tab:model_components} lists the concrete per-model components of each surveyed system, organised by architectural family. Each row reports the vision backbone, the input resolution or page-rendering setting, the LLM backbone, and the adaptors or projectors that connect them. Where a model lists multiple backbones, slashes denote interchangeable choices reported in the paper, while commas separate components used together in distinct roles. The four families differ markedly in how they assemble these parts. Page-to-document transformers train smaller LLM backbones such as T5 variants or DocFormerv2 paired with custom layout-aware projectors and document tokens. Backbone-centric MLLMs reuse standard vision-language stacks such as Qwen2.5-VL, mPLUG-Owl2, and SigLIP variants, and add light projection or compression modules, often updating only LoRA adapters or compressors. Retriever-generator pipelines decouple a frozen retriever such as ColPali, ColQwen, or VisRAG from a frozen LVLM and train only the scorer, fuser, or reranker. Agentic pipelines mostly orchestrate frozen frontier VLMs through prompting and tool use, with parameter updates limited to a few systems that fine-tune their agent backbone via SFT or GRPO.

\subsection{Open-Source Code Releases}
\label{app:open-source}
Table~\ref{tab:model_open_source} lists the surveyed MP-VRDU models that publish their implementation through a public GitHub repository. Sixteen of the 36 papers link such a repository directly in their source PDF. A further three systems, DocLens, RAG-DocVQA, and VDocRAG, distribute through \texttt{github.io} project pages rather than full code repositories, and MACT states that source code will be released publicly without yet providing a URL. The remaining surveyed papers do not announce open-source releases at all.

\section{More Dataset Details}
\label{dataset_detailed}

The surveyed papers reference 31 distinct pretraining datasets, 64 fine-tuning datasets, and over 70 benchmark datasets. Eight datasets are introduced by papers within the corpus (MP-DocVQA, M3DocVQA, ViDoSeek, OpenDocVQA, Doc-750K, PaperPDF, CoR-Dataset, and the Leopard-Instruct / MHDocVQA / MP-DocStruct1M / MP-DocReason51K family), while the remainder are inherited from single-page VRDU, general vision-language pretraining, or text-only long-document QA. Information of the most significant and commonly used datasets are compiled in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_survey}.

\subsection{Pretraining Datasets}
Pretraining at scale is confined to backbone-centric models, since retriever--generator and agentic-pipeline papers inherit pretrained backbones and skip this stage. Three recipes recur. The first is OCR and layout self-supervision, used by Hi-VT5 \cite{tito2023hierarchical} on OCR-IDL \cite{biten2022ocridl} (200K pages), Arctic-TILT \cite{borchmann2025arctic} on CCpdf \cite{turski2023ccpdf}, and Texthawk2 \cite{yu2024texthawk2} on IIT-CDIP \cite{lewis2006iitcdip}. The second is document-structure pretraining over synthesised QA, supplying weakly-supervised text-to-position alignment through DocStruct4M (4M cross-domain samples) \cite{hu2024mplug}, the purely multi-page MP-DocStruct1M (1.1M samples) \cite{hu2025mplug2}, the scientific corpus PaperPDF (1.1M bilingual PDF/QA pairs) \cite{xie2024wukong}, and Doc-750K (758K QA / 3.1M images / 251K conversations) \cite{duan2025docopilot}. The third is generic VLM pretraining followed by document continue-pretraining, in which a web-scale vision--language corpus such as Cauldron \cite{laurenccon2024building} is combined with document-specific data, as in Leopard \cite{jia2024leopard} and Texthawk2 \cite{yu2024texthawk2}. The field is converging on this third recipe, with token-level structure prediction over synthesised QA displacing simple OCR-text masked-language-modelling as the dominant document pretext task.

\subsection{Fine-Tuning and Instruction Datasets}
Two strategies dominate fine-tuning. Task-mixture fine-tuning, used by mPLUG-DocOwl2 \cite{hu2025mplug2}, Leopard \cite{jia2024leopard}, Docopilot \cite{duan2025docopilot}, and DocR1 \cite{xiong2026docr1}, aggregates a broad pool of public sources such as DocVQA \cite{mathew2021docvqa}, ChartQA \cite{masry2022chartqa}, InfographicVQA \cite{mathew2022infographicvqa}, DUDE \cite{van2023dude}, MP-DocVQA \cite{tito2023hierarchical}, TAT-DQA \cite{zhu2022tatdqa}, MultiHiertt \cite{zhao2022multihiertt}, FUNSD \cite{jaume2019funsd}, OCR-VQA \cite{mishra2019ocrvqa}, and TextVQA \cite{singh2019textvqa} into a unified multi-task mix. Capability-targeted fine-tuning instead curates a single corpus, typically synthesised by a strong LVLM, as in Leopard-Instruct (925K text-rich multi-image instances) \cite{jia2024leopard}, the CoR-Dataset (26K reasoning-trace QA pairs) \cite{guo2026end}, MoLoRAG's logical-relevance corpus \cite{wu2025molorag} (5.5K GPT-4o-synthesised pairs over MMLongBench-Doc \cite{ma2024mmlongbench} and LongDocURL \cite{deng2025longdocurl} PDFs), and DocDancer trajectories \cite{zhang2026docdancer}. Reinforcement and preference learning is emerging at small scale through DocR1's evidence-page-guided GRPO \cite{xiong2026docr1} and CoR's DPO pairs \cite{guo2026end}. A defining feature of this stage is heavy reuse of evaluation datasets for training; MP-DocVQA appears in 15 fine-tuning mixes and 17 evaluation suites, DUDE in 12 and 16, and DocVQA in 9 and 14 respectively (full per-paper attributions in Table~\ref{tab:datasets_used}), with implications discussed in \S\ref{challenges}.

\subsection{Benchmark Datasets}
The benchmark stack spans three page-count tiers, catalogued in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_survey} with per-paper adoption in Table~\ref{tab:datasets_used}. Single-page benchmarks (DocVQA \cite{mathew2021docvqa}, InfographicVQA \cite{mathew2022infographicvqa}, ChartQA \cite{masry2022chartqa}, VisualMRC \cite{tanaka2021visualmrc}, TextVQA \cite{singh2019textvqa}, FUNSD \cite{jaume2019funsd}, and CORD \cite{park2019cord}) originated the field and now mainly serve as sanity checks. The mid-length default suite is dominated by MP-DocVQA \cite{tito2023hierarchical} (5,928 documents, 8.3 pages avg, 46K Qs; 17 papers), DUDE \cite{van2023dude} (5,019 documents, 5.7 pages avg, 41.5K Qs; 16 papers), and SlideVQA \cite{tanaka2023slidevqa} (2,619 decks of $\sim$20 slides, 14.5K Qs; 13 papers). The long-document frontier is dominated by MMLongBench-Doc \cite{ma2024mmlongbench} (135 documents, 47.5 pages avg, 1,082 Qs; 17 papers), often abbreviated ``MMLongBench'' in body text yet distinct from the unrelated Wang et al.~\citeyear{wang2025mmlongbench} benchmark of the same shorthand, which no surveyed model uses. LongDocURL \cite{deng2025longdocurl} (85.6 pages avg; 10 papers) and DocBench \cite{zou2024docbench} (3 papers) act as close peers, and M-LongDoc \cite{chia2025m} pushes length further. Domain-specific evaluation (TAT-DQA \cite{zhu2022tatdqa}, Kleister \cite{stanislawek2021kleister}, CUAD \cite{hendrycks2021cuad}, BRIDGE \cite{xiang2026bridge}, PaperPDF \cite{xie2024wukong}) and open-domain retrieval evaluation (M3DocVQA \cite{cho2025m3docvqa}, ViDoSeek \cite{wang2025vidorag}, OpenDocVQA \cite{tanaka2025vdocrag}) each appear in only one to three papers, while KGP \cite{wang2024knowledge} relies on text-only multi-hop suites \cite{yang2018hotpotqa, trivedi2022musique}. Evidence-aware evaluation is growing through UDA's PaperTab and FetaTab sub-tasks \cite{hui2024uda} and the evidence-page metrics of MMLongBench-Doc, LongDocURL, DocR1 \cite{xiong2026docr1}, and DocLens \cite{zhu2025doclens}.
